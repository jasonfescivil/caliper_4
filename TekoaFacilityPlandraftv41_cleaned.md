INTRODUCTION

This Wastewater Treatment Plant Facility Plan (Facility Plan) provides a comprehensive assessment and strategic roadmap for the City of Tekoa, Washington's wastewater infrastructure over the next 20 years. It thoroughly evaluates the existing collection, treatment, and disposal systems, identifies current deficiencies and future needs, and recommends the most cost-effective and compliant path forward. The analysis includes a detailed comparison of potential improvement scenarios against a no-action alternative to guide the City's decisions on essential wastewater system investments.

In addition, this report fulfills the Washington Administrative Code (WAC) 173-240-050, which requires an up to date General Sewer Plan in order to consider changes to the existing system such as the ones proposed in this Engineering Report. 

Regulatory Requirements Summary

This Facility Plan is developed in accordance with the requirements outlined in Washington Administrative Code (WAC) Chapter 173-240, "Submission of Plans and Reports for Construction of Wastewater Facilities," particularly WAC 173-240-060 which details the required engineering report content. It also considers design standards and guidance from the Washington State Department of Ecology's (Ecology) "Criteria for Sewage Works Design" (Orange Book), relevant surface water quality standards (WAC 173-201A), groundwater quality standards (WAC 173-200), and federal secondary treatment regulations. The table presented in the preliminary section cross-references specific WAC 173-240-060 requirements to their location within this report.

Planning Requirements

Wastewater systems in Washington State follow a comprehensive regulatory framework that spans from initial planning through operational monitoring. During the planning phase, the Department of Ecology maintains oversight authority by requiring approval of general sewer plans, engineering reports, and construction plans and specifications before any construction activities commence. The specific content requirements and sequencing for these submittals are established in WAC 173-240 and the Criteria for Sewage Works Design (Orange Book, CSWD). Environmental review under the State Environmental Policy Act (SEPA) must be completed during the planning phase prior to obtaining Ecology approvals, with additional State Environmental Review Process (SERP) or National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) requirements applying when federal funding is involved.

Design Requirements

Design standards for wastewater facilities must demonstrate reasonable consistency with CSWD guidelines, though Ecology recognizes alternative design references from professional organizations including the Water Environment Federation (WEF), American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE), American Public Works Association (APWA), and Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) for design review purposes. Projects incorporating water reclamation components must additionally comply with WAC 173-219 (Purple Book) and require coordination with both Ecology and the Department of Health.

Permitting Requirements

The permitting framework encompasses multiple regulatory programs depending on the discharge type and receiving environment. National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permits are required for surface water discharges, with Ecology serving as the delegated permitting authority. State Waste Discharge (SWD) permits govern discharges to ground and certain discharges to sewers. Facilities producing reclaimed water operate under permits issued pursuant to RCW 90.46 and WAC 173-219, which are frequently combined with NPDES or SWD permits for administrative efficiency. Construction of outfalls and utility crossings in waters of the United States requires Section 401 water quality certification to accompany Corps of Engineers Section 404 or Section 10 permits, with additional local floodplain and shoreline permits potentially applicable, as described in the discussion on alternatives in Chapter 6.

Operational Requirements

Operational monitoring requirements are specified within NPDES and SWD permits, with laboratory accreditation rules and applicable exceptions detailed in WAC 173-216 [20][7]. 

Regulatory Precedence

When regulatory conflicts arise, Water Quality Standards established under WAC 173-201A take precedence and may require effluent limits more stringent than technology-based standards. In cases where multiple requirements apply, facilities must meet the most restrictive standard. For projects receiving Ecology funding, SERP allows SEPA documentation to satisfy NEPA-like requirements, though EPA retains NEPA oversight authority for projects funded through the Water Infrastructure Finance and Innovation Act (WIFIA) program [14][2].

Watershed Considerations

The Tekoa WWTP discharges treated effluent to Hangman Creek, a tributary within the Spokane River watershed. Water quality in Hangman Creek is a significant consideration due to its status and potential impacts on downstream uses and stream health. Given the importance of the creek to the ecological health of the watershed, several studies have been conducted by Ecology on the creek’s health, the background of which is important to understand due to its effect on the Tekoa WWTP NPDES permit. The studies and their consequences are summarized in the sections below.

2009 Hangman Watershed Water Quality Improvement Report

The Hangman Creek Watershed Water Quality Improvement Report, published in June 2009 by the Washington State Department of Ecology. The impetus for this report stems from Hangman Creek and its tributaries being listed on Washington State's 303(d) list of impaired water bodies due to failing to meet state water quality standards for fecal coliform, temperature, and turbidity. The federal Clean Water Act mandates the development of a Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) for each pollutant and water body on this list to identify and clean up polluted waters. Additionally, there was regional interest in reducing Hangman Creek's phosphorus contribution to the Spokane River, as phosphorus loads are associated with suspended sediments and turbidity and contribute to depressed dissolved oxygen levels in the Spokane River and Lake Spokane.

The objectives of this TMDL report were to develop a plan to meet water quality standards for these three pollutants in Hangman Creek and its tributaries. The study involved characterizing fecal coliform bacteria, heat, and suspended sediment loading. Using the results of the study, TMDL allocations for these parameters were set, and an implementation strategy for adhering to the parameters was put forward. Study included reviewing historical data, engaging local agencies, and integrating fieldwork for technical analysis. Implementation objectives focused on community involvement, developing a local plan, and securing technical and financial assistance to meet the updated standards. The study used historical and field data collected by the Spokane County Conservation District (SCCD), Ecology, and other agencies which had been collected over recent decades. For fecal coliform, temperature, and TSS, statistical and modeling methods were used to generate the recommended load allocations

The results indicated widespread water quality issues in the Hangman Creek watershed, but many of the impacts were resulting from sources unrelated to the Tekoa WWTP. For fecal coliform, many areas and tributaries frequently exceeded criteria, primarily due to storm events (runoff Sources of the exceedances were diverse, including livestock access, malfunctioning septic systems, historically inadequate WWTP disinfection (now largely corrected), wildlife, and stormwater runoff. A 72% bacteria load reduction was deemed necessary at the mouth of Hangman Creek during the July-September critical period, with other areas requiring 10% to 85% reductions. Temperature consistently exceeded the 7-day average daily maximum (7DADM) 17.5 degrees Celsius criterion over many reaches of the Creek during the critical June-August period. Current riparian shade was significantly below the "system potential", which is the estimated shade from mature native vegetation under undisturbed conditions. WWTPs discharging during the critical summer months (Tekoa and Spangle) received specific monthly maximum effluent temperature limits, while those discharging to wetlands or with seasonal restrictions were assigned "no discharge" waste load allocations during these months. Turbidity and TSS were identified as long-standing problems, stemming from naturally erosive streambanks and upland soils destabilized by poor infrastructure and agricultural practices. Although TSS and turbidity showed some recent decline, aquatic communities remained poor, with frequent intense events causing lethal or sub-lethal effects on fish. Statistical modeling predicted a significant reduction in adverse markers if "full protection scenario" (for instance, conservation agriculture, riparian buffers, streambank erosion control) were implemented. WWTPs were considered insignificant sources of TSS, though the watershed’s conditions as characterized in the report are important for understanding the regulatory environment moving forward.

The report issued several key recommendations for water quality improvement. A Water Quality Implementation Plan (WQIP) was to be developed within one year of TMDL approval (see section 2.4.3 for the improvement plan published in 2011), detailing specific actions. Most of the plan’s recommendations focus on nonpoint source pollution and integrate Best Management Practices (BMPs) that reduce erosion, runoff, and livestock impacts, and increase stream shading. However, the report also recommends enforcing stringent effluent limits for Tekoa WWTP. For temperature, ongoing temperature monitoring was required for all WWTPs to refine effluent limits, and permit holders were advised to evaluate their systems to prevent thermal impacts. 

2011 Hangman Creek Watershed Fecal Coliform, Temperature, and Turbidity TMDL Water Data Summary Report

The "Hangman Creek Watershed Dissolved Oxygen, pH, and Nutrients Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) Study Data Summary Report" outlines the foundational data collection for a comprehensive water quality assessment. The impetus for this study arose because previous TMDL evaluations for Hangman Creek, which addressed fecal coliform bacteria, temperature, and turbidity, lacked sufficient data to assess the role of phosphorus in pH and dissolved oxygen (DO) criteria violations. Furthermore, several segments of Hangman Creek and its tributaries were identified on Washington State's 303(d) list of impaired waters for not meeting state water quality standards for pH and DO, necessitating this data collection effort to fill those gaps. The watershed, covering nearly 700 square miles and primarily characterized by dryland farming. The objectives of the study were to refine the existing landscape model of the Hangman Creek watershed and to develop a numerical water quality model for DO and pH analysis, specifically targeting the low-flow summer period using data from collected surveys.

The methodology involved the Washington State Department of Ecology collecting water quality and streamflow data from September 2008 through September 2009. Data collection encompassed synoptic surveys during the summer and fall low-flow seasons, including time-of-travel tests, pH and DO monitoring, and productivity monitoring. The study also monitored nutrient and chemical reference conditions across the watershed's four ecoregions and assessed Washington/Idaho border nutrient loads. Field data collected included pH, conductivity, dissolved oxygen, temperature, and flow. Laboratory analyses were performed for chloride, total suspended solids, alkalinity, various nitrogen and phosphorus species, total organic carbon, dissolved organic carbon, biochemical oxygen demand, chlorophyll, and volatile organic matter. Data collection adhered to the Hangman Creek Watershed Dissolved Oxygen and pH Quality Assurance Project Plan and Ecology's standard collection procedures. The analytical framework for the report focused on summarizing the collected data and performing a quality control and quality assurance analysis. The data presented in the report were intended to comprise part of an ongoing detailed analysis, including water quality modeling and statistical analysis, which were used to establish pollutant load and waste load allocations for Hangman Creek and its tributaries.

The results of this data summary report consist of all collected field measurements, comprehensive laboratory data, continuous temperature plots, streamflow measurements. Because the report was an accounting of a data collection and reporting effort, recommendations are not provided in this "Data Summary Report". 

2011 Hangman Creek Watershed Fecal Coliform, Temperature, and Turbidity TMDL Implementation Plan

The Hangman (Latah) Creek Watershed Fecal Coliform Bacteria, Temperature, and Turbidity Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) Water Quality Implementation Plan outlines a comprehensive strategy to restore water quality in the Hangman Creek Watershed. The impetus for this plan stems arose from the fact that streams within the Hangman Creek Watershed were shown to be significantly impaired by excess fecal coliform, turbidity, and elevated water temperatures. The primary objective of this Water Quality Implementation Plan was to expand upon the recommendations of the preceding TMDL report by detailing the roles and responsibilities required to bring the impaired streams into compliance with water quality standards for bacteria, temperature, and turbidity. Additionally, the plan recommend activities that will reduce nutrient contributions, particularly phosphorus, which is a significant concern for the Spokane River and Lake Spokane Dissolved Oxygen TMDL (to which Hangman Creek is a tributary), and is also linked to dissolved oxygen and pH impairments in Hangman Creek. 

As previously touched upon, the analytical framework for this plan is based on the Total Maximum Daily Load (TMDL) approach, mandated by the federal Clean Water Act (CWA) for all 303(d) listed impaired waters. A TMDL is a quantitative assessment that determines the maximum amount of a pollutant a water body can receive without violating water quality standards (the "loading capacity") and then allocates this load among various sources. This allocation includes wasteload allocations (WLAs) for discrete point sources (like municipal or industrial discharge pipes) and load allocations (LAs) for diffuse nonpoint sources (such as agricultural or urban runoff). The report outlines a an implementation strategy, expanding on the pollutant reduction requirements and allocations established in the initial TMDL document approved in 2009. Town of Tekoa Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) - Regulatory Compliance and Water Quality Implementation

The Town of Tekoa Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP), one of six National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permitted facilities discharging to surface waters within the Hangman Creek Watershed, plays a specific role in the comprehensive Water Quality Implementation Plan. Its effluent discharges directly into Hangman Creek. For fecal coliform bacteria, the TMDL assessment identified the Tekoa WWTP as the only point source requiring a reduction from its then-current NPDES permit limits. The TMDL established a specific 78% reduction target for Tekoa WWTP to achieve a wasteload allocation, down from a previously calculated load which was based on 2003-2004 data. However, the report also notes that, based on the most recent monitoring records at the time of publication, the Tekoa WWTP was already in compliance with this fecal coliform target reduction. This has by-in-large remained true in the intervening years outside a sparse set of violations.

Of key importance to operation of any propose wastewater treatment facility, the Tekoa WWTP faces specific numeric wasteload with respect to temperature allocations for effluent discharge during the critical summer months of June, July, and August. These WLAs specify maximum effluent temperatures of 18.2°C for June, 21.5°C for July, and 17.7°C for August. This is a distinct requirement, as several other WWTPs in the watershed have a "No discharge" requirement during these months. Furthermore, the Hangman Creek reach above the Tekoa WWTP has a significant need for riparian restoration, requiring a 50% increase in effective shade to reach system potential vegetation, indicating existing temperature challenges in the surrounding stream environment.

For TSS, the report states that the current NPDES turbidity limits for all WWTPs, including Tekoa, are considered adequate to protect water quality and require no further reduction. Overall, as with other WWTPs in the watershed, when the NPDES permit for the Town of Tekoa WWTP is reissued, it will contain the wasteload allocations established in this TMDL. The Town is encouraged to work closely with its Ecology permit manager to ensure all necessary actions are addressed in its monitoring and permit requirements for TMDL compliance. Additionally, the Town of Tekoa, like other towns in the watershed, is advised to plan for necessary improvements to meet these limits and to prepare for potential nutrient limits in the future when its permits are reissued, as nutrient reduction is a broader goal related to the Spokane River and Lake Spokane Dissolved Oxygen TMDL.

2020 Tekoa Wastewater Treatment Plant Dissolved Oxygen, pH, and Nutrients Receiving Water Study

The Tekoa Wastewater Treatment Plant Dissolved Oxygen, pH, and Nutrients Receiving Water Study was primarily driven by the aging infrastructure of the City of Tekoa's wastewater treatment plant (WWTP), which was originally built in 1950 with significant upgrades in 1974 and 1990. Further impetus was the plant's compliance schedule to meet effluent temperature limits by July 2024, as established by the 2009 Hangman Creek Watershed TMDL. Crucially, preliminary data from 2009 had indicated that nutrient discharges from the Tekoa WWTP might be contributing to DO and pH impairments in Hangman Creek. The objectives of the 2017 field study, which focused on nutrients, pH, dissolved oxygen, and algal eutrophication during May-October critical period, were to determine the effluent nutrient load limits necessary to protect pH and DO in Hangman Creek and to provide essential information for Tekoa's facility planning efforts. The study aimed to present its field investigation findings, offer a modeling analysis of effluent nutrient impacts, and recommend specific effluent nutrient limits.

The analytical approach for study initially planned to use existing models standard for river water quality, which successfully calibrated hydrodynamics and temperature. The results of the study revealed several key findings which revealed consistent impacts on pH and DO were observed downstream of Tekoa WWTP during summer low-flow periods, with pH frequently exceeding the water quality standard. Unlike many small streams, suspended algae played a crucial role in determining summertime pH and DO levels in upper Hangman Creek, attributed to the exceptionally slow water movement through deep, wide pools. Tekoa WWTP was identified as the predominant source of soluble reactive phosphorus and contributed the majority (at times over 90%) of both SRP and dissolved inorganic nitrogen (DIN) during the critical low-flow period. Algae growth was primarily nitrogen-limited, though phosphorus was also found to be co-limiting. Environmental factors like extreme seasonal streamflow variability (effluent from the Tekoa plant comprised up to 10-15% of downstream flow during summer), clear water allowing light penetration, and warm summer temperatures created a critical season for nutrient-driven algal growth and subsequent pH and DO impacts.

Based on these findings, the study offered several recommendations to Ecology to implement stringent effluent nutrient load limits for the Tekoa WWTP for DIN and TP. These limits are recommended to be applied annually during the critical season from June through October, as determined by flow and temperature conditions. Summary and Implications

The series of studies conducted on the Tekoa Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) and the broader Hangman Creek watershed have profound relevance to the feasibility proposed plant modifications, revealing critical environmental challenges and leading to stringent regulatory requirements. 

This extensive body of research should critically inform the planning for a new wastewater treatment plant for the City of Tekoa, especially given the existing facility's aging infrastructure and urgent need for repair, upgrade, or replacement. The new plant must be designed to meet the highly restrictive effluent nutrient load limits for dissolved inorganic nitrogen (DIN) and total phosphorus (TP), which are applicable annually during the critical season from June through October. These limits represent a small fraction of current nutrient discharges, and meeting the proposed limits will necessitate either advanced treatment technologies or periods of diverted effluent flow such as land application or seasonal storage. Furthermore, the new facility must comply with the effluent temperature limits established by the 2009 TMDL which were brought into effect in July 2024. Options for thermal reduction such as seasonal storage, land application, or infiltration trenches should be thoroughly investigated. Planned modifications must accommodate the poor dilution factor of Tekoa's effluent in Hangman Creek during summer low flows, where wastewater can comprise up to 10-15% of the downstream flow.

City of Tekoa's Shoreline Master Program (SMP)

The City of Tekoa's Shoreline Master Program (SMP) is a document that aligns the City’s shoreline policy with the Washington State Shoreline Management Act (SMA). Its primary goal is to provide a balanced use of the City’s shoreline by integrating environmental protection, public access, and water-oriented development. The SMP covers Latah Creek, land within 200 feet of the ordinary high water mark (OHWM), floodways, contiguous 100-year floodplains extending 200 feet inland of a floodway, and associated wetlands.

Core principles of the SMP include:

Environmental Protection: Safeguarding natural resources like land, vegetation, wildlife, and aquatic life.

Public Access: Preserving and enhancing opportunities for the public to enjoy shorelines.

Water-Oriented Uses: Prioritizing uses that are water-dependent, water-related, or water-enjoyment.

No Net Loss of Ecological Functions: A central tenet requiring all uses and developments to maintain the current aggregate total of shoreline ecological functions, with impacts identified and addressed.

The SMP is structured to define its purpose, vision, shoreline jurisdiction and five environment designations (Urban Conservancy, Shoreline Residential, Shoreline Parks, High Intensity, and Aquatic), general policies, specific use and modification policies, and administrative procedures. Each environment designation has specific management policies and use preferences. A key regulatory tool is the "Shoreline Use and Modification Table" which classifies activities as permitted, conditional, or prohibited within each designation.

Archeological and Historical

Recent projects have served as opportunities for archeological and historical review of the treatment plant site and facilities nearby. Most recently, a cultural survey was carried out in preparation for the construction of the influent lift station. The primary goal of the cultural resource survey was to identify and assess any archaeological or cultural resources within the proposed construction area of the influent lift station. This survey was undertaken to fulfill the cultural resources regulatory requirements mandated by the Department of Ecology and the Spokane Tribal Historic Preservation Office before beginning construction activities.

The search revealed four previously recorded historic properties within a 1-mile radius of the project area, primarily consisting of railroad-related features. The subsurface survey of the area yielded no pre-contact archaeological materials. However, one historic artifact, a 4-inch nail, was found in one STP, and modern debris (concrete fragments) was recovered from another. The soil profile generally indicated disturbed conditions, likely from the initial construction of the wastewater treatment plant.

The Spokane Tribe of Indians Preservation Program concluded that the proposed project was unlikely to have adverse effects on significant cultural resources based on the survey findings. Tekoa Area Geography and Landscape

(WAC 173-240-050(f))

Tekoa is in an agricultural area of Eastern Washington known as the Palouse. It is situated approximately 40 miles southeast of Spokane. The area surrounding Tekoa is characterized by characteristic rolling hills of the Palouse region. The primary industry related to the area's geography is agriculture. The geography, particularly the terrain and presence of Hangman Creek and Little Hangman Creek, influences local drainage patterns which are relevant to stormwater management and potential infiltration/inflow into the sewer system.

The area soils are predominantly silt or loess, which accumulated from glacial deposits overlying the area’s basalt flow formations, along with the siltstones and sandstones of the Latah Formation. Buttes such as Tekoa Mountain, formed from islands of siltite and quartzite jutting above the basalt flows, are composed of harder, less permeable rock; these features generally lead to lower water infiltration rates, increased surface runoff, and the development of thin, rocky soils often unsuitable for agriculture. Understanding the areas geological setting and the soil types and underlying that geology is important for evaluating infiltration potential and site suitability for infrastructure construction related to treatment.

Tekoa Water System Description 

The City of Tekoa owns and operates a Group A public water system (DOH ID: 828707) that serves the residents within the city limits. The system includes 3 active wells, 1 Irrigation well, 1 decommissioned well, 1 storage reservoirs, and a network of distribution and transmission mains. The system operates with a single pressure zone. 

Water Sources

The City’s water supply comes from two chlorinated ground water wells, both with vertical shaft turbine pumps and shelters. There is an also an irrigation well at the golf course and a decommissioned well as part of the City’s water system.

Well 1 (AGG-171) or the “North Well” across from city hall at 400 Washington St was an original well for the city built in the late 1920’s – early 1930’s. It was decommissioned in 2011 after a partial collapse. It was a 6” Diameter, 176ft deep well producing 210 gallons per minute (gpm). 

Well 2 (AGG-172) or the “South Well” across from city hall at 400 Washington St was an original well for the city built in the late 1920’s-1930’s was decommissioned in 2009. It was a 4” diameter, 167ft deep well producing 100 gpm

Well 3 (AGG-173) or the “Truax Well” at 402 Line St at the corner of Truax St and Line St serves as the City’s primary Well. It was built in 1978. It is a 16” diameter 1400ft deep well producing 900 gpm. 

Well 4 (AAU-279) constructed in 2006 is located at the city’s golf course at 202 Golf course Rd and serves as an irrigation well for the City’s golf course. It is an 8” diameter 180ft deep well producing 100 gpm. 

Well 5 (APS-592) is across from City Hall at 400 400 Washington St was constructed in 2011 and commissioned in 2012. This well was drilled adjacent to AGG-171 and, replacing it and well AGG-172. This well is used as the City’s backup/emergency well It is a 12” diameter 303ft deep well producing 280 gpm.

All active wells are in the Columbia River Basalt Group aquifer system providing a source of high-quality groundwater with minimal treatment requirements beyond chlorination for disinfection.

Population Projection Methodology

To support long-term infrastructure planning, population projections for the City of Tekoa were determined using three different strategies. The first one was developed using historical data from 1990-2024, the second used historical data from 2009-2024, and the third used historical data from 1970-2020. Additionally, WAOFM’s county wide projections were also applied directly to Tekoa’s population. The projections extend over a 5-year period to 2030 and a 20-year period to 2045 to assist in facility capacity planning, particularly for the wastewater treatment infrastructure.

Data Sources

The first analysis looked at decennial census data from WAOFM from 1990-2024 as 1990 was the low point in Tekoa’s population after its Peak in 1910. The second analysis looked at WAOFM’s intercensal data set from 2009 to 2024, as Tekoa saw a population decline in the late 2000’s that bottomed out the following year in 2010. A third analysis used data from 1970 to 2024 as that was the data range available through the National Historic Geographic Information Service for the Census Tracts within Spokane County. These three data sources were used in addition to WAOFM’s Medium population forecast as its 2025 forecasted number, since it most closely matched their 2020 postcensal data and estimated population for 2024. The projections resulting from each method were then compared to an extended linear trendline of Tekoa’s population trendline. 

Method 1: WAOFM’s growth projection

To project future population trends, projections from WAOFM were used. This state agency produces three growth forecasts at the individual county level. The medium growth forecast was chosen as its estimate of 2025 population most closely matched the 2024 postcensal population estimate for Whitman County. These growth rates were then applied to Tekoa’s population using the equation Population = Prev. Pop. * (1+growth rate)YRS. 

Method 2: Decennial Census Data

To project future population trends, historical population for Tekoa was analyzed using Microsoft Excel. A scatter plot and linear trendline were created to capture Tekoa’s relatively steady increase in population since 1990. The best-fit trendline was then used to forecast future population values. By substituting the target years (e.g. 2030 and 2045) into the equation, population projections were generated. This method offers a data-driven approach to long-term planning, ensuring that future trends are estimated based on observed historical patterns, rather than assuming a growth rate. This gave an equation of Population = 1.4949(year)-2204.8 with a statistical correlation value (R2) value of 0.4069. Despite this weak linear correlation, it still tracked well with WAOFM’s medium growth forecast percentages for Whitman County.

 Load and Flow Variations

Seasonal

Wastewater flows and characteristics in Tekoa exhibit seasonal variations typical of Eastern Washington communities with lagoon systems. Flows are generally higher during the late fall, winter, and spring months due to increased precipitation, snowmelt, and higher groundwater levels contributing to I&I. Conversely, flows are typically lower during the drier summer months. Influent wastewater temperatures are lower in winter, which can reduce the biological treatment efficiency of the lagoon system, potentially impacting BOD and ammonia removal.

Diurnal

Daily flow patterns (diurnal variations) are typical of predominantly residential communities of the type served by the Tekoa Wastewater Treatment Plant. Flows are lowest during the early morning hours (approx. 1 AM - 5 AM) and typically peak twice: a morning peak between 7 AM - 10 AM and an evening peak between 6 PM - 9 PM, corresponding to periods of highest water use in homes.

ERU Calculations

The amount of wastewater generated by a typical single-family home, the Equivalent Residential Unit (ERU) is a fundamental parameter in wastewater planning, representing the daily hydraulic contribution generated by a single home or dwelling. The ERU flow was estimated in multiple ways to as a check against error and to account for the strengths and weaknesses of each method. In facility plans, the ERU concept is used to normalize contributions from various types of connections (residential, commercial, institutional) to a common unit, which simplifies the process of projecting future wastewater flows based on anticipated growth in housing and commercial development. Because of the contribution from I&I and other factors such as the larger variety of uses which make up the commercial and industrial units, residential units are the simplest basis to use as a common basis.

Calculating daily minimum flows is necessary to ensure that self-cleansing velocities are maintained within the gravity portions of the sewer collection system, preventing the deposition of solids and subsequent blockages, odor issues, and corrosion. It is also important for the proper operation of pumps (to avoid excessive short-cycling which can damage equipment) and some treatment processes that may be sensitive to very low flow conditions or require a minimum hydraulic throughput.

To estimate a "peak hour minimum flow," or more accurately, the lowest instantaneous flow expected during the diurnal cycle on a minimum flow day, the minimum average daily flow rate is considered. Even during the day with the lowest total flow, there will still be diurnal variations with very low flows typically occurring in the late-night/early-morning hours. <<check>> This can be estimated using a minimum diurnal factor (for instance, {{ Minimum_Diurnal_Factor_Value, for instance, 0.25 to 0.5 }} times the average flow on that minimum day). This minimum instantaneous flow is the critical condition for assessing if velocities in sewers are sufficient to prevent solids deposition, especially in flatter gradient pipes. 

Ensuring adequate cleansing velocity (typically around 2 feet per second when flowing at least half full for sanitary sewers) at minimum flow conditions is important because it helps maintain the operational efficiency and longevity of the collection system. It prevents the buildup of solids, which can lead to reduced pipe capacity, accelerated corrosion from septic conditions, nuisance odor generation, and an increased need for costly and disruptive sewer cleaning operations. 

Dry Weather Flow

Average Dry Weather Flow (ADWF) is defined as the total wastewater flow entering the treatment facility during an extended dry period, typically lasting 7 to 14 consecutive days, when direct rainfall-induced inflow is negligible. This flow consists of the true sanitary flow (Qs) generated by residential, commercial, and institutional users, combined with groundwater infiltration (QGWI) that may seep into the collection system through pipe defects, joints, or manholes, especially when groundwater levels are seasonally high. It explicitly excludes significant, direct commercial or industrial process discharges that are not part of the baseline sanitary load, if any. 

The assumption of "no additional inflow" during dry weather conditions is critical for isolating the sanitary and groundwater infiltration components. Inflow is primarily associated with direct stormwater entry into the sanitary sewer system, commonly from sources such as improperly connected sump pumps, roof leaders, foundation drains, or cross-connections with storm drains, all of which are activated by precipitation events. By analyzing flow during periods without rainfall, these direct inflow contributions are minimized or eliminated, allowing for a more accurate assessment of the baseline wastewater generation and persistent infiltration. <<check>>

Quantitatively, ADWF can be represented as:
ADWF = Qs + QGWI
Where:

ADWF = Average Dry Weather Flow (gpd)

Qs = Base Sanitary Flow from domestic, commercial, and institutional sources (gpd)

QGWI = Groundwater Infiltration (gpd)

Wet Weather Flow

Rainfall-Derived Infiltration and Inflow (RDII) is the extraneous surface and groundwater that enters the sanitary sewer system exclusively as a direct consequence of precipitation events. It is composed of two primary components:

Inflow: Stormwater that enters the sewer system rapidly and directly. Common sources include direct connections like roof leaders, yard drains, catch basins connected to the sanitary sewer, sump pumps improperly discharged to the sanitary sewer, holes in manhole covers, or structurally failed pipes that allow surface water to pour in. 

Infiltration: Groundwater that enters the system more slowly through defects below the ground surface, such as cracks in pipes, deteriorated pipe joints, faulty service connections, and leaky manholes. The rate of this infiltration is often exacerbated by a rise in the groundwater table due to prolonged or intense rainfall saturating the surrounding soil. 

RDII is a major concern in wastewater management as it can consume valuable hydraulic capacity in both the collection system pipes and the WWTP. Excessive RDII can lead to sanitary sewer overflows, cause basement backups, reduce treatment efficiency at the WWTP due to dilution and hydraulic overloading, and significantly increase pumping and treatment operational costs.

Mathematically, the total Wet Weather Flow (WWF) during a storm event can be expressed by considering RDII along with the base flow components:
WWF = Qs + QGWI + QRDII
Where:

WWF = Total Wet Weather Flow (for instance, in gpd) during the event.

Qs (gpd) = Base Sanitary Flow (gpd) – the component from user discharges.

Wet Weather Flow (WWF) is qualitatively defined as the significantly increased and often rapidly fluctuating flow observed at the WWTP during and immediately following a rainfall event. This flow includes the normal ADWF components (sanitary flow and baseline groundwater infiltration) plus the additional, often substantial, volume contributed by RDII. The magnitude, timing (how quickly the flow responds to rain), and duration of the WWF response depend on numerous factors. These include rainfall intensity, total rainfall volume, and duration; antecedent moisture conditions (how saturated the ground was before the storm); the physical condition and age of the sewer system (more defects lead to more I&I); soil types and geology; groundwater levels; the topography of the service area; and the extent of directly connected impervious surfaces.

In evaluating potential methods for managing treated wastewater effluent from the City of Tekoa facility, the analysis was performed in light of two reports issued by Ecology which are likely to inform the next NPDES permit to be issued on the facility. During the restricted discharge period (June-October), various land-based application and treatment systems were considered. A preliminary screening was conducted using the USDA Soil Resource Report for the farmland surrounding Tekoa (See Appendix X) to assess fundamental site suitability based on soil types, topography, and other limitations. This analysis compared site characteristics against the general requirements for different land application methods as outlined in the Washington State Department of Orange Book and other recommended guidance documents, including the Design Criteria for Municipal Wastewater Land Treatment Systems for Public Health Protection (DOH, 1994) and principles from the Guidelines for Preparation of Engineering Reports for Industrial Wastewater Land Application Systems (Ecology 93-36).

Both methods rely on the soil-plant matrix for nutrient uptake and additional water treatment. The USDA report indicated that Map Unit 64 (Palouse silt loam) was rated "Not limited" for irrigation and only "Somewhat limited" for slow rate treatment, with the limitation being minor slope constraints primarily affecting surface (non-sprinkler) application. This soil type generally possesses moderate permeability and water holding capacity suitable for crop uptake, aligning with characteristics common for sites using slow rate systems. Map Unit 87 (Schumacher silt loam) was also rated "Somewhat limited" for both uses. While Map Unit 87 is potentially usable, noted limitations include moderate depth to bedrock (40-60 inches) and slower water movement. According to the recommended guidance documents, these factors necessitate careful site-specific design, and likely involving implementing lower hydraulic and nutrient loading rates than Unit 64 to prevent breakthrough to groundwater or perched saturation above the bedrock prior to effective treatment. The slopes in Map Unit 87 would also require careful consideration for irrigation design to minimize runoff. While these units (64 and 87) show some potential, the limited available acreage of Unit 64 and the specific limitations of Unit 87 necessitate rigorous field investigation to confirm suitability and determine actual design loading rates. Map Unit 1010 (Caldwell-Thatuna complex), although rated "Somewhat limited", presents significant challenges related to high water tables and flooding risk, making it considerably less desirable and likely requiring extensive, potentially cost-prohibitive controls (such as underdrainage) inconsistent with typical land treatment feasibility for small systems.

Other land application methods were excluded based on incompatibilities between their requirements and the site's prevailing soil and topographic characteristics. Disposal of Wastewater by Rapid Infiltration was deemed unsuitable due to "Very limited" ratings across all map units. Rapid infiltration systems require highly permeable soils, such as sands and gravels, capable of accepting high hydraulic loading rates. The dominant soil types identified in the Tekoa area are characterized by moderate to moderately slow permeability. Applying high volumes of water typical of rapid infiltration to these finer soils would inevitably lead to prolonged surface ponding and runoff.

Overland Flow Treatment was also eliminated during the screening process. This method relies on using slowly permeable soils (typically clays or clay loams) on carefully graded, uniform slopes (generally 2-8%) to allow wastewater to flow in a thin sheet across the vegetated surface, which allows for treatment through biological action and sedimentation before collection as runoff. The Tekoa area soils are predominantly silt loams, lacking the required low permeability for sheet flow generation. Furthermore, the regional topography features many hills and buttes, with variable and often steeper slopes than ideal for controlled overland flow. The combination of unsuitable soil permeability and challenging topography makes overland flow technically infeasible for this location.

Therefore, based on this initial screening assessment integrating USDA soil data with established land application/treatment principles and criteria from Ecology and DOH guidance, only Slow Rate Treatment and Disposal by Irrigation demonstrate sufficient potential compatibility with site conditions to be carried forward for detailed hydrogeologic investigation and comparative analysis within this Facility Plan.

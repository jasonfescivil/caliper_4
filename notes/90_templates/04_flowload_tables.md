Below is a defensible framework you can apply to develop population projections and translate them into design flows and loadings for a new municipal wastewater treatment plant serving a Washington State community. Each step cites applicable Washington Department of Ecology (Ecology) criteria (AKART/WAC), U.S. federal requirements, and recognized industry standards and practices.

1) Regulatory and planning basis (AKART/WAC and federal context)
- AKART and consistency with Ecology’s Criteria for Sewage Works Design (CSWD): Ecology requires reasonable consistency with CSWD in design reviews; AKART appears in CSWD abbreviations and is the overarching basis to apply all known, available, and reasonable methods in the design and documentation [27 §173‑240‑040(2), p.4; 19, p.12].
- Required submittals and content: The engineering report must be sufficiently complete to support plans and specs without substantial changes, include present and future wastewater quantity and quality, and discuss I/I and actions to address it [21 §173‑240‑060(2)-(3)(c),(g), p.6; 10, p.48].
- General sewer plan and population projections: Provide historic population and 20‑year projections consistent with local land use planning; use OFM or official sources; align with the comprehensive plan [10, p.48; 25 G1‑3.2, p.41].
- Federal wet‑weather/SSO context: CMOM/SSO control programs are widely used to eliminate overflows; design/rehab is often driven by capacity assurance and wet‑weather performance [2, p.17–19; 35 §2.3.1.5, p.30].
- Federal secondary treatment/percent removal: Adjustments to percent removal limits are allowed for less concentrated influent and to encourage I/I control, using federal definitions of excessive/non‑excessive I/I [30 §133.103(a),(d),(e), p.78–79; 34, p.79].

2) Population and service forecasts (document methods and scenarios)
Use multiple defensible methods and reconcile them with local plans. Ecology expects 20‑year projections and consistency with land use planning [10, p.48; 25, p.41].

- Land use and dwelling unit (DU) method (preferred for accuracy):
  - Obtain current zoning/general plan densities and persons/DU from the planning department (e.g., low density ≤4 DU/acre at 3.0 persons/DU; medium 4–10 DU/acre at 2.8; high >10 DU/acre at 2.5). Adjust for vacancy, transients, and group quarters (hotels, dorms, seasonal) [9, p.55].
  - Subdivide the sewershed into subareas; compute population at the most downstream key points (manholes/pump stations) for the beginning and end of the design period [9, p.55].
- Per capita “return ratio” method:
  - Develop wastewater per capita as a fraction of metered water use using long‑term water and sewer flow data, considering groundwater infiltration (GWI) and conservation trends [4, p.14].
- Employment/nonresidential method:
  - Forecast nonresidential flows by land use/employment and apply unit flows per land use; include major institutions/industrial sources case‑by‑case [7 Table 3‑1 Steps, p.51; 18, p.50].
- Equivalent Dwelling Unit (EDU), regression, cohort methods:
  - Ecology allows methods other than per capita if documented (e.g., based on water records, measured flows, “other methods”) and submitted for Ecology review/approval [1 C1‑3.3.1, p.209]. If you use EDU, regression, cohort‑survival, or econometric methods, document inputs/assumptions and reconcile to local plans (not specifically prescribed in CSWD).
- Existing systems:
  - For rehab/expansion, base design on measured flows and observed characteristics; provide an I/I mitigation plan if excessive [1 C1‑4.1 first paragraph, p.209; 5 G2‑1.2.3, p.96].

Deliverable: Provide low/medium/high population scenarios by subarea and summed systemwide for year 0 and year 20, with methods and data sources.

3) Base wastewater flows (BWF) and average day design flow
- Residential per capita: For new systems, use 100 gal/capita·d (gpcd), assumed to cover normal infiltration; add industrial/institutional/commercial components as applicable [5 Table G2‑2 (Dwellings), p.96; 2 Ten‑State summary, p.17].
- Alternative unit flows: Table G2‑2 provides design flows for schools, restaurants, motels, trailer courts, etc.; add these to residential flow as appropriate [5 Table G2‑2, p.96].
- Develop average flows manhole‑by‑manhole: Sum subarea average flows to produce Qavg at each key point; compute beginning of service (Qavg1) and end‑of‑horizon (Qavg2) average flows [16, p.68; 6, p.52].

Equation (end of planning period average day flow):
Qavg2_total = 100 gpcd × Pop_end + Σ(nonres flows from Table G2‑2) + normal I/I (embedded) [5, p.96; 2, p.17].

4) Infiltration/Inflow (I/I) allowances (GWI and RDII)
- Normal I/I is included in the 100 gpcd for new systems; add an allowance where conditions are unfavorable (e.g., high groundwater, older sewers, illicit connections) using local data [1 C1‑3.3.1 and C1‑3.3.3, p.209; 2, p.17].
- GWI allowances: Industry practice includes 1,000 gal/acre·d; some require up to 2,000 gal/acre·d; determine site‑specifically from monitoring, ideally in high groundwater season (early spring) [4 GWI, p.14].
- RDII: Quantify via flow monitoring and modeling; RDII is the wet‑weather response above DWF; consider manhole cover inflow, roof/foundation drains, illicit connections [4 RDII, p.14; 17, p.14].
- Example municipal practice: Cincinnati adds 1,000 gal/acre·d I/I to peak flow and computes design wet‑weather flow components (BWF, GWI, RDII) using a design storm and SCS rainfall distribution [3, p.19].
- WAC/CSWD expectations: Discuss I/I problems and actions; for existing systems, provide a plan to eliminate/handle excessive I/I to avoid treatment impairment [21 §173‑240‑060(3)(g), p.6; 5 G2‑1.2.3, p.96].

5) Peaking factor derivation and design capacity flows
- Peak hour (capacity) design flow: Generally, sewers and downstream facilities are designed to carry at least the peak hourly flow. Ecology directs using design average daily flow with a peaking factor from Ten‑State Standards (Figure C1‑1); the peaking factor must be ≥2.5. Agencies may use a local peaking curve based on actual data [1 C1‑3.3.2, p.209].
- Ten‑State/Harmon equation: PF = (18 + √P) / (4 + √P), where P is population in thousands; note these PF may be higher than observed in modern systems but may be mandated [13, p.194].
- Point‑by‑point peaking: Apply peaking factors at each manhole to generate peak 1‑hour flows (Qmax) for capacity design and minimum flows (Qmin) for self‑cleansing evaluation [16, p.68; 6, p.52].
- Maximum day and maximum week: Use observed data to establish MDDF and MWDF as controlling process design conditions; Ecology requires the plant to treat all flows up to MWDF for secondary treatment, and up to MDDF for plants with toxics limits [5 General Considerations, p.96; 8 (4)–(6) and Table G2‑1, p.94].

Equations:
- Peak hour design flow (PHDF) = Qavg2_total × PF (≥2.5), plus RDII as appropriate for wet‑weather capacity design [1 C1‑3.3.2, p.209; 17, p.14].
- Maximum day dry‑weather flow (MDDF): Determine from observed diurnal factors or local data per CSWD/WEF guidance; CSWD emphasizes using observed rates during significant periods for process design [5, p.96]. If local diurnal peaking is available, apply a daily PF to Qavg2 to derive MDDF.
- Maximum week dry‑weather flow (MWDF): Establish from monitoring/records; used for biological reactor design per Table G2‑1 [8 Table G2‑1, p.94].

6) Seasonal adjustments and safety/contingency factors
- Seasonal variation: GWI is higher in wet spring high‑groundwater season; outdoor water use is lower then, aiding BWF/GWI separation [4 GWI, p.14]. Maximum month loads/flows can occur in warm or cold seasons; design should reflect seasonal temperature and load variability [11, p.214].
- Safety factor and parallel trains: Ecology requires justification of an appropriate safety factor ensuring compliance at maximum flow/loading; if peak hour treatment plant flow and solids loading are ≥3× average dry weather, include multiple parallel trains [8 (5)–(6), p.94].

7) Translating projections into design flows
- Step A: Compute Qavg2_total (end‑of‑horizon average day):
  Qavg2_total = 100 gpcd × Pop_end + Σ(nonres unit flows from Table G2‑2) + site‑specific GWI allowance if needed [5 Table G2‑2, p.96; 1 C1‑3.3.1, p.209; 4, p.14].
- Step B: Compute MDDF and MWDF:
  - Use observed/analogous communities’ diurnal and weekly peaking for dry weather (process control per Table G2‑1) [5, p.96; 8, p.94].
- Step C: Compute peak hour flow (PHDF):
  PHDF = Qavg2_total × PF(Harmon or local) with PF ≥2.5; include RDII for wet‑weather capacity if required [1 C1‑3.3.2, p.209; 13, p.194; 5, p.96].
- Step D: Map flows to unit process design per CSWD Table G2‑1 (e.g., clarifiers at PHDF, bioreactors at MWDF/MDDF, pumping at PIDF/PHDF) [8 Table G2‑1, p.94].

8) Translating projections into design loadings (BOD5, TSS, TN, TP, NH3‑N)
- Preferred approach: Use local influent monitoring (at least one full year; more is recommended) to derive representative concentrations and loads for BOD5, TSS, TKN/NH3‑N, and TP; include industrial contributions and seasonal/diurnal variation in the engineering report [10, p.48].
- If local data are not yet available for new systems:
  - Apply Ecology’s per‑capita design loadings for new sewage works from Table G2‑2:
    - BOD5: 0.2 lb/capita·d
    - TSS: 0.2 lb/capita·d
    - Flow: 100 gpcd
    These per‑capita values are for “Dwellings” and are intended as design bases for new systems [5 Table G2‑2, p.96].
  - For nitrogen and phosphorus: CSWD/WEF direct per‑capita N and P values are not specified in the cited sections. Use site‑specific monitoring or, if necessary for interim planning, concentration‑based assumptions from recognized design examples. For example, MOP 8 provides a documented design case with average influent concentrations of BOD5 140 mg/L, TSS 90 mg/L, TKN 30 mg/L, and TP 7 mg/L (illustrative only; verify for your service area) [11, p.214]. Ammonia‑N must be determined from local data (not specified in CSWD tables here) [10, p.48].
- Mass load calculations:
  - Per‑capita method (BOD5, TSS): Load_BOD5 = 0.2 lb/cap·d × Pop_end; Load_TSS = 0.2 lb/cap·d × Pop_end [5 Table G2‑2, p.96].
  - Concentration method (for nutrients or if using concentration‑based BOD/TSS): Load = Q × C × 8.34 (lb/d), where Q is in MGD and C in mg/L. Use Qavg2 for average loads; apply appropriate peaking for maximum day/week loads with observed seasonal/diurnal factors [5, p.96; 11, p.214].
  - Integrate nonresidential contributions using Table G2‑2 unit loads where applicable (e.g., restaurants) [5, p.96].
- Seasonal maxima: Establish maximum month/week loads by applying seasonal concentration factors or directly from monitoring (MOP 8 shows maximum month mass loads distinct from average) [11, p.214].

9) I/I and federal permit considerations (linking design to permitting)
- Excessive/non‑excessive I/I thresholds (for permit percent removal considerations): federal regulations identify thresholds (e.g., 275 gpcd total flow; 40 gpcd or 1,500 gal/in‑dia·mi·d for infiltration) in evaluating percent removal adjustments; these are not design targets but inform I/I control and permit compliance strategy [30 §133.103, p.78–79; 34, p.79].
- SSO/CMOM: Design capacity assurance, O&M, and wet‑weather performance are central to eliminating SSOs under state NPDES implementation [2, p.17–19; 35, p.30].

10) Summary of defensible design workflow (to include in the engineering report)
- Define planning horizon and service area; compile land use, zoning, DU densities, persons/DU, vacancy, transients [9, p.55; 10, p.48].
- Develop population forecasts by subarea using DU method; cross‑check with return‑ratio/water use and employment‑based estimates; document any alternative methods (EDU, regression, cohort) for Ecology review [1 C1‑3.3.1, p.209; 4, p.14; 7, p.51].
- Compute average day flows: 100 gpcd × population + nonresidential flows (Table G2‑2); add GWI allowance if warranted by local conditions/monitoring [5, p.96; 1, p.209; 4, p.14].
- Quantify I/I: Plan and conduct flow monitoring (preferably in wet spring) to separate BWF and GWI; characterize RDII via modeling for wet‑weather capacity where applicable [4, p.14; 17, p.14; 3, p.19].
- Derive peaking factors: Use Ecology/Ten‑State PF (PF ≥2.5) by population or local PF curve; compute PHDF; establish MDDF/MWDF from observed diurnal/weekly patterns [1 C1‑3.3.2, p.209; 13, p.194; 5, p.96].
- Translate to loads: Use Table G2‑2 per‑capita loads for BOD5/TSS; derive TN/TP/NH3‑N from local data or (for planning) concentration assumptions from recognized references; develop average, maximum day/week, and peak hour flow‑linked loads [5, p.96; 11, p.214; 10, p.48].
- Apply seasonal and safety factors: Reflect higher spring GWI and seasonal max loads; justify safety factor; include parallel trains if peak hour flows/loads ≥3× ADW [4, p.14; 8, p.94].
- Map flows to process units per CSWD Table G2‑1 (e.g., clarifiers at PHDF; biological reactors at MWDF/MDDF) [8 Table G2‑1, p.94].
- Document compliance: Address I/I control plan for existing systems, SSO avoidance, and NPDES context; ensure report is sufficiently complete per WAC [5, p.96; 21, p.6; 27, p.4].

Notes and limitations
- Per‑capita nitrogen, phosphorus, and ammonia loadings are not specified in the cited CSWD tables; use local monitoring per WAC/CSWD or recognized design concentrations from literature examples, with clear caveats [10, p.48; 11, p.214]. If such data are unavailable, state “unknown pending monitoring” for ammonia and adopt conservative planning assumptions only for preliminary sizing, to be refined with measured data.
- Regression, cohort, and EDU methods are acceptable under CSWD as “other methods” if documented and approved by Ecology (not specifically defined in CSWD) [1 C1‑3.3.1, p.209].

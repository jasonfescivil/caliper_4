# AKART Analysis Playbook for a Municipal Wastewater Facility (Regulator-Facing)

Purpose: Provide an end-to-end, regulator-aligned roadmap to demonstrate All Known, Available, and Reasonable methods of prevention, control, and treatment, culminating in an approvable engineering report, plans, and implementation pathway.

Regulatory anchors:
- Washington engineering report and plans/specifications requirements, including content, alternatives analysis, outfall analysis, I/I discussion, sludge/residuals, future needs, staffing/testing, costs, and SEPA/NEPA compliance [20], [8], [12], [21].
- Early coordination with the lead and nonlead agencies and opportunities to streamline reclaimed water-related reports [2].
- Sewer system evaluation for I/I and sequencing of treatment works design relative to I/I control [5], [10].
- Hydraulic evaluation, flow monitoring, and modeling practices to establish defensible baselines and evaluate alternatives [19], [4].  
- Model selection and capabilities (e.g., SWMM blocks) to support collection system hydraulics and pollutant transport evaluations [1].

Note on scope: This playbook is generic and content-only; it embeds decision gates, deliverables, QA, and traceability controls so a separate project team can attach project-specific data and exhibits.

---

## Phase 1. Scoping

Objectives
- Define the problem statement and regulatory drivers (treatment, conveyance, disposal/reuse) [20], [8], [21].
- Confirm lead/nonlead agency roles and early coordination expectations [2].
- Identify the planning horizon, study area, and facilities in scope (collection, pumping, treatment, outfall/land application, storage) [20], [8], [12], [21].
- Establish AKART objectives and decision criteria aligned to water quality standards, SEPA/NEPA, and adopted water quality management plans [20], [8].

Scope elements
- Project description; present and proposed service area maps; location of facilities; topography; hydrologic context; receiving waters; outfalls [20], [8], [21].
- Initial stakeholder map and regulatory touchpoints (e.g., aquatic lands coordination for outfalls) [21], [2].
- Initial I/I problem framing and potential overflows/bypasses [8], [20].
- Industrial discharges and potential pretreatment implications [8], [20].

Decision gate
- Gate: Scoping approval by owner and regulatory partners (alignment on objectives, criteria, scope-of-work, submittal milestones) [2], [20].

Deliverables
- Scoping memorandum with regulatory alignment and schedule.
- Study plan: data acquisition, monitoring, modeling, QA, and stakeholder engagement approach [19], [4], [2].

Notes footer
- Assumptions: Roles, scope boundaries, and regulatory touchpoints are as stated pending agency confirmation.  
- Limitations: Scoping subject to refinement after baseline data QA and initial field findings.  
- To Be Refined: Planning horizon, performance targets, and study extents based on validated baselines.

## Phase 2. Data and Baseline

Objectives
- Compile and validate the physical, operational, hydraulic, and water quality baseline to support AKART screening and detailed evaluation.

Data assembly
- System inventory and mapping: physical features, utilities, existing sewer connections, future extensions, environmental constraints [9].  
- Service area and population/land use baselines; wastewater quantity and quality projections; uncertainty recognized in forecasts [11], [18].  
- Flow, rainfall, and groundwater data; monitoring program designed for hydraulic evaluation and I/I analyses; protect against bad data; assess data quality [4], [19].  
- I/I diagnostic framework (e.g., patterned interviews, map study, flow partitioning, dry vs wet weather, selective tests) [5].  
- Treatment works baseline: unit processes, receiving waters, water quality standards context, outfalls and mixing zone considerations [20], [21].
- Industrial waste streams impacting collection/treatment/solids [8], [20].

Modeling foundations
- Select/justify hydraulic-hydrologic and water quality models; consider SWMM blocks for runoff, kinematic wave routing, and dynamic routing/pollutant transport as appropriate [1]; establish intended applications (master planning, hydraulic analyses, regulatory compliance) [19].

Decision gate
- Gate: Baseline and QA sign-off (validated datasets, monitoring summaries, model build approach, performance criteria) [19], [4], [18].

Deliverables
- Baseline technical memorandum; geospatial inventory; monitoring plan and QA results; modeling plan (calibration/verification strategy) [19].  
- Preliminary problem statements for capacity, I/I, overflows, effluent/outfall compliance, industrial loadings [8], [20], [21].

Notes footer
- Assumptions: Data sources-of-truth defined; model selection fit-for-purpose.  
- Limitations: Provisional baselines pending calibration/verification and agency feedback.  
- To Be Refined: Forecasts and inflow components subject to sensitivity testing.

## Phase 3. Alternatives Generation (AKART Toolbox)

Objectives
- Systematically identify prevention, control, and treatment alternatives across the collection, treatment, and disposal/reuse continuum.

Alternative categories
- Collection/conveyance: I/I reduction, capacity relief, storage, pumping, operational controls; rehabilitation options and programmatic measures [4], [19], [5].  
- Treatment: process upgrades, reliability improvements, industrial pretreatment needs [8].  
- Disposal/reuse: outfall improvements with mixing analyses; land application variants with soils/geohydrology evaluations when applicable [21], [8], [12].  
- Monitoring/control: metering, instrumentation, controls to meet performance objectives [12], [19].  
- Phased implementations and staged development to ultimate condition [23].

Decision gate
- Gate: Alternatives catalog concurrence (completeness against regulatory and performance objectives) [8], [21], [23].

Deliverables
- Alternatives catalog with conceptual descriptions, performance hypotheses, and data/modeling needs.

Notes footer
- Assumptions: Alternatives reflect AKART breadth and regulatory pathways.  
- Limitations: Concepts subject to feasibility testing and stakeholder input.  
- To Be Refined: Site-specific constraints and integration sequencing.

---

## Phase 4. Screening

Objectives
- Narrow to a shortlist using transparent criteria grounded in regulation, feasibility, and performance.

Screening criteria
- Regulatory compliance potential: water quality standards, mixing zones, permits, pretreatment, overflows/bypasses controls [8], [21], [20].  
- Technical feasibility and constructability; staging potential; O&M considerations; safety [23], [24], [12].  
- Capacity and risk reduction for wet weather and overflow remediation [4], [19].  
- Environmental and community impacts; SEPA/NEPA pathways [8], [20].  
- Cost reasonableness for planning-level comparison; life-cycle framing [21].  
- Clear documentation of reasons alternatives are unacceptable, per WAC report requirements [8], [20].

Decision gate
- Gate: Shortlist endorsement by owner and agencies; agreement on evaluation methods and performance metrics [2], [20].

Deliverables
- Screening matrix and shortlist; documentation of excluded alternatives with reasons [8], [20].

Notes footer
- Assumptions: Criteria reflect regulatory expectations and owner objectives.  
- Limitations: Planning-level costs and feasibility only.  
- To Be Refined: Metrics and thresholds during detailed evaluation.

---

## Phase 5. Detailed Evaluation

Objectives
- Provide defensible, calibrated, and QA’d analyses of shortlisted alternatives to support selection.

Analytical work
- Hydraulic/hydrologic modeling: calibration/verification; assessment of capacity, surcharge/backwater, overflow risk, and pump station performance; selection of modeling block(s) appropriate to dynamics [19], [1].  
- I/I and wet-weather performance: flow/rainfall analyses; use of control areas when applicable; event and continuous assessments [4], [19].  
- Outfall and receiving water: mixing zone analysis, calibrated to field conditions, covering critical flow/loading situations [21].  
- Treatment process performance: degree of treatment required; basic design data; sizing calculations; expected efficiencies and effluent character [8], [20].  
- Industrial pretreatment implications and requirements [8].  
- Residuals/biosolids: management plan, options evaluation, regulatory alignment [8], [21].  
- Staffing/testing: facility classification, staffing sufficiency, roles, and lab/testing needs [21].  
- Costs: capital and O&M over project life; total annual cost and present worth; methods and accuracy statements [21].

Uncertainty, sensitivity, and QA
- Perform uncertainty and sensitivity analyses for forecasts and key assumptions; consider alternative forecasts for sensitivity [11], [18].  
- Data validation, quality checks, and protection against bad data; assessment of rainfall/flow data quality [19], [4].  
- Document model calibration/verification methods and acceptance criteria [19].

Decision gate
- Gate: Draft engineering report content review with agencies to confirm completeness and sufficiency for decisions [20], [2].

Deliverables
- Detailed evaluation technical memorandum(s); draft engineering report sections with WAC-required content [20], [8], [21].

Notes footer
- Assumptions: Model configurations and boundary conditions as documented.  
- Limitations: Residual uncertainties summarized; additional data needs identified.  
- To Be Refined: Final design criteria following preferred alternative selection and any required pilot/demonstration evidence [12].

## Phase 6. Reconciliation and Preferred Alternative

Objectives
- Integrate technical, regulatory, and stakeholder inputs; select the preferred AKART alternative and phasing.

Key steps
- Reconcile tradeoffs across compliance, performance, constructability, O&M, costs, and stakeholder considerations [8], [21], [24].  
- Confirm I/I control approach such that finalization of treatment plant design is prudent and reliable [10].  
- Define staged implementation path consistent with ultimate system configuration [23].

Decision gate
- Gate: Preferred alternative decision; direction to finalize the engineering report and initiate permitting/design [20].

Deliverables
- Preferred alternative memorandum; phasing plan; change log documenting any substantial changes that would require engineering report amendment if applicable [2].

Notes footer
- Assumptions: Selected alternative meets performance standards within stated constraints.  
- Limitations: Further refinement during final design and permitting.  
- To Be Refined: Operational strategies and contingency provisions.

---

## Phase 7. Recommendation and Record of Decision

Objectives
- Produce the regulator-facing engineering report and decision record.

Engineering report contents (per WAC)
- Owner and representative; project description; service area maps [20].  
- Present and future wastewater quantity and quality; degree of treatment required; receiving water, standards, and compliance approach [20].  
- Type of treatment process; alternatives evaluated with reasons for rejection; basic design data and sizing; expected unit and plant efficiencies; anticipated effluent [8], [20].  
- Outfall analysis; mixing zone analysis calibrated to field conditions; identification of aquatic lands considerations and compliance [21].  
- I/I, overflows, and bypasses discussion with proposed controls [8], [20].  
- Industrial waste provisions and any pretreatment requirements [8].  
- Method of final sludge disposal; alternatives considered [8], [21].  
- Provisions for future needs [8], [21].  
- Staffing and testing requirements; facility classification alignment [21].  
- Costs and expenses over life of project; total annual cost and present worth; methods and accuracy [21].  
- Compliance statements for SEPA/NEPA and adopted water quality management plan [20], [8].  
- Land application-specific geohydrologic and soils information when applicable [8], [12].

Decision gate
- Gate: Engineering report approval (precedent to plans and specifications) [20], [22].

Deliverables
- Final engineering report; SEPA/NEPA documentation; outfall/mixing zone analysis; pretreatment program summary as applicable [20], [21], [8].

Notes footer
- Assumptions: Report contains sufficient detail to proceed to plans and specifications without substantial changes [20], [2].  
- Limitations: Any substantial change will trigger an amendment process [2].  
- To Be Refined: Site-specific constructability and access constraints during design development.

## Phase 8. Design and Implementation Planning

Objectives
- Translate the approved engineering report into approvable plans/specs and a constructible, operable project.

Plans and specifications (per WAC)
- Repeat basic design criteria; explain deviations from the report if any; plan/section drawings; site plan; schematics; hydraulic profile under maximum flows; instrumentation/controls/sampling; general operating procedures [12].  
- Submit for approval before construction; preceded by approved engineering report [8], [22].

Constructability and phasing
- Construction phase execution aligned to plans/specs; consider staging, bypassing, and ROW/utility coordination to minimize disruption [16], [24], [23].  
- Interim operation planning to maintain service during construction [8].  
- Phased implementation aligned to staged development strategy from the engineering report [23].

O&M and performance management
- Prepare/update operation and maintenance documentation per chapter requirements; staffing/testing readiness [15], [21].  
- Post-project monitoring and documentation of effectiveness (e.g., overflow remediation, I/I reduction, capacity improvements, maintenance performance, regulatory compliance) [4].

Residuals and outfall execution
- Implement residuals/biosolids plan and outfall/diffuser as basic units of the system with field-calibrated basis [21], [8].

Industrial pretreatment
- Implement necessary pretreatment provisions for significant industrial sources [8].

Re-evaluation triggers
- Establish triggers tied to monitoring outcomes, regulatory changes, or material deviations that could constitute substantial changes requiring engineering report amendment [2], [4], [19].

Decision gate
- Gate: Plans/specifications approval; proceed-to-construct authorization [8].  
- Gate: Construction closeout and declaration of completion per chapter process; initiate operations [15].

Deliverables
- Approved plans/specs package; construction QA/QC plan; O&M procedures; monitoring and reporting plan; change management controls [12], [15], [21].

Notes footer
- Assumptions: Construction means/methods can meet staged access and safety needs.  
- Limitations: Final site constraints may affect sequencing.  
- To Be Refined: Commissioning tests and performance verification protocols.

## Quality Assurance: Data Validation, Uncertainty, Sensitivity

Program elements
- Data QA/QC: screening, calibration data sufficiency, protection from poor-quality datasets; document data lineage and QA results [19].  
- Flow/rainfall/groundwater data resolution and duration consistent with objectives; use control areas where appropriate [4].  
- Modeling QA: calibration/verification methodology; acceptance criteria; documentation of configuration and parameters [19].  
- Forecast uncertainty and sensitivity testing; use alternative forecasts to gauge robustness [11], [18].  
- Independent checks on critical calculations and outfall analyses calibrated to field conditions [21].

Notes footer
- Assumptions: QA thresholds reflect intended application and regulatory standards.  
- Limitations: Residual uncertainty disclosed; contingency paths identified.  
- To Be Refined: Additional monitoring or testing if QA thresholds are not met.

## Stakeholder Checkpoints

Engagement points
- Early coordination with Ecology and Health to streamline engineering report alignment and parallel requirements [2].  
- Scoping concurrence, baseline/QA sign-off, alternatives shortlist, draft engineering report review, preferred alternative confirmation, final engineering report approval, plans/specs approval [2], [20], [8].  
- SEPA/NEPA milestones for public and interagency review where applicable [8], [20].  
- Outfall siting coordination where aquatic lands are involved; document compliance and communications [21].

Notes footer
- Assumptions: Stakeholder roles and schedule align with agency processes.  
- Limitations: Milestones may adjust to accommodate public review timelines.  
- To Be Refined: Engagement artifacts and comment response matrices.

## Defensibility and Traceability

Controls
- Assumptions register and sources-of-truth index (maps, monitoring datasets, calibration files, permits, standards) tied to WAC-required report content [20], [8], [21].  
- Change log and version control across scoping, baselines, models, alternatives, and report sections; flagging of changes that may be substantial under the engineering report standard [2], [20].  
- Documentation of alternatives rejected with reasons to satisfy report requirements [8], [20].  
- Crosswalk matrix linking engineering report items to exhibits and underlying analyses; maintain consistency into plans/specs [20], [12], [22].  
- Calibration and field-validation records for outfall analyses and key models [21], [19].

Notes footer
- Assumptions: This record is the authoritative trace of decisions and data.  
- Limitations: External datasets subject to their owners’ updates.  
- To Be Refined: Final cross-references post-approval.

## Implementation Planning Focus Areas

Phasing and constructability
- Stage development toward ultimate condition; coordinate ROW/utilities; plan interim operations and bypassing [23], [24], [8].

Operations and maintenance
- Staffing/testing plan aligned with facility classification; operating procedures and training [21], [12], [15].

Residuals management
- Biosolids/residuals plan with alternatives and regulatory alignment [8], [21].

Energy, sustainability, resilience
- Consideration headings reserved for project-specific evaluation aligned with future needs planning; document rationale within alternatives and phasing [8], [21].  
  - Assumptions: Will be evaluated consistent with regulatory and owner objectives.  
  - Limitations: No prescriptive requirements cited herein.  
  - To Be Refined: Criteria and metrics during detailed evaluation.

Re-evaluation triggers
- Monitoring-based triggers; regulatory changes; material deviations constituting substantial changes requiring report amendment [2], [4], [19].

Notes footer
- Assumptions: Implementation aligns to approved report and permits.  
- Limitations: Field conditions may necessitate design adjustments consistent with approvals.  
- To Be Refined: Commissioning and post-implementation verification details.

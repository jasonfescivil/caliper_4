To estimate a "peak hour minimum flow," or more accurately, the lowest instantaneous flow expected during the diurnal cycle on a minimum flow day, the minimum average daily flow rate is considered. Even during the day with the lowest total flow, there will still be diurnal variations with very low flows typically occurring in the late-night/early-morning hours. <<check>> This can be estimated using a minimum diurnal factor (for instance,  {{ Minimum_Diurnal_Factor_Value, for instance,  0.25 to 0.5 }} times the average flow on that minimum day). This minimum instantaneous flow is the critical condition for assessing if velocities in sewers are sufficient to prevent solids deposition, especially in flatter gradient pipes. 
Ensuring adequate cleansing velocity (typically around 2 feet per second when flowing at least half full for sanitary sewers) at minimum flow conditions is important because it helps maintain the operational efficiency and longevity of the collection system. It prevents the buildup of solids, which can lead to reduced pipe capacity, accelerated corrosion from septic conditions, nuisance odor generation, and an increased need for costly and disruptive sewer cleaning operations.

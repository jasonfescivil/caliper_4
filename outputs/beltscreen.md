Planning-level workflow to evaluate “belt” thickening options at a 1,000‑person WWTP (Washington State focus; U.S.-wide applicability)

0) Confirm objectives, scope, and regulatory baseline
- Define whether you need thickening only (e.g., for digester feed or storage control) or dewatering and product quality for end use/disposal. Thickening reduces downstream hydraulic/solids loads and transportation costs [DOE CSWD S‑2.1, p.590–592; S‑2.1 intro and Table S‑1, p.592] and is one step in an overall solids management plan [DOE CSWD S overview, p.585].
- Washington: Biosolids/sludge program is more stringent than federal in several ways; a statewide Biosolids General Permit (5‑yr) is administered by Ecology; engage the Regional Biosolids Coordinator early because permitting can be time‑consuming [DOE CSWD S‑1.2.2 State, p.588–590]. Solids management components are reviewed as part of facility plans; Water Quality Program consults with Solid Waste Program [DOE CSWD S‑1.2.3 Plan Review, p.590].
- Federal: Part 503 applies to land application, pathogen and vector attraction reduction, pollutant limits, recordkeeping/reporting; general provisions apply to all use/disposal practices [EPA Part 503 Guide, Subpart A, p.17]. Part 503 reporting is required only for POTWs ≥1 mgd, serving ≥10,000 people, or Class I facilities [EPA Guide, Who Must Report, p.26]. Washington still requires an annual biosolids report by March 1 for all TWTDS [WAC 173‑308‑295, p.37].
- Roles in WA: “Person who prepares biosolids” must ensure compliance with WAC 173‑308 and permits [WAC 173‑308‑090, p.12]. Applying to land must comply with WAC 173‑308 and applicable permits [WAC 173‑308‑110, p.12]. Transporters need a spill plan and vehicle cleaning; WUTC rules also apply [WAC 173‑308‑100, p.12].
- Planning/approvals (WA): Follow Ecology’s sequence—General Sewer Plan, Engineering Report, then Plans & Specs; all require Ecology approval before construction [DOE CSWD G1‑2.2, p.33; G1‑3.1/3.2, p.41]. Solids management must be reasonably consistent with CSWD [DOE CSWD Introduction, p.5].

1) Quantify existing and future solids and return flows
- Characterize solids streams (primary, WAS, mixed, digested); determine incoming % solids, volumes, and diurnal/weekly variability.
- If no test/pilot data, use DOE CSWD “rough design guidelines” (mass loading by solids source) for gravity thickening as a screening tool and to scale test units; examples include primary, WAS (air/oxygen/extended aeration), aerobically digested, etc. [DOE CSWD Table S‑1, p.592].
- Plan to sample/analyze in accordance with EPA‑specified methods when biosolids quality determinations are needed (metals, pathogens, vector attraction) [EPA Guide, Analytical Methods, p.154].

Washington note: Biosolids must have preliminary treatment to remove garbage; the state rule has a minimum screening standard and a final product quality standard for recognizable garbage (see Chapter T1) [DOE CSWD S‑2 intro, p.590]. Screenings/grit/scum are “solids” managed under WAC 173‑350 (solid waste rule) [DOE CSWD S‑1.1.1, p.588].

2) Define end use/disposal pathway and compliance targets
- Beneficial use land application (Class A/B/EQ/PC/CPLR/APLR): identify pathogen reduction and vector attraction reduction standards to be met prior to or concurrent with thickening/dewatering/stabilization [DOE CSWD S‑1.2.1, p.589–590; EPA Guide, Recordkeeping/Reporting table, p.58].
- Disposal to MSW landfill: must meet landfill rules (173‑351 WAC/40 CFR 258); sewage sludge used as daily cover is disposal; intermediate/final cover may be beneficial use if conditions are met [WAC 173‑308‑300, p.37].
- Set up recordkeeping/reporting consistent with pathway (EQ/PC/CPLR/APLR) [EPA Guide, Recordkeeping/Reporting, p.58]; in WA submit annual report by Mar 1 [WAC 173‑308‑295, p.37].

3) Shortlist technology configurations (“belt” options) and how they fit your plant
- Gravity belt filtration (Gravity Belt Thickener, GBT): DOE CSWD S‑2.1.3 covers purpose, design considerations (mixing and chemical feed; air handling/odor control; O&M), and advantages/disadvantages [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.3 headings, p.587].
- Belt filter press (BFP, dewatering): DOE CSWD S‑2.1.4 covers purpose; upstream needs (e.g., gravity belt thickener; circular drum screens with exterior cloth); design considerations (chemical feed; air/odor control; O&M); advantages/disadvantages [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.4, p.587]. Belt processes require chemicals to aid thickening [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.5/C.2 line noting belt filter chemical need, p.605].
- Rotary drum/circular drum screens (RST/RDT): noted as upstream of BFPs [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.4.2.b, p.587].
- Other comparators (for context/costing): DAF thickening, centrifuges, gravity thickeners, drying beds, bag dewatering are also candidate methods to include in a planning-level alternatives analysis [DOE CSWD S‑2.1 list, p.590–592; p.592].

U.S.-wide note: EPA and WEF emphasize selecting stabilization/thickening with end use in mind; evaluate sidestream impacts, odor, safety, flexibility, and the ability to consistently meet biosolids quality [WEF MOP8 Ch. 23, p.233–234]. Consider sustainability/energy in alternatives evaluation [WEF Facilities Design—Design advances, p.9].

4) Establish performance expectations and testing plan
- Recognize that manufacturer performance varies; Ecology recommends bench-scale tests, and ideally pilot testing (e.g., lease a unit) to set design/operating parameters and verify product % solids and capture [DOE CSWD S‑2.1 intro, p.590–592].
- During testing, document polymer type/dose, belt speed, feed rates, filtrate quality/return flow loads, odor control needs, and housekeeping.

5) Residuals/side-stream handling and integration with the liquid train
- Plan for filtrate/centrate/supernatant routing to appropriate points preceding the secondary process; include sampling and capacity to handle organic/nutrient loads from return flows (analogous return flow guidance for drying beds emphasizes returning upstream of influent sampling and accounting for organic loads in design) [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.6.C.6, p.605–607].
- Screenings and grit handling/disposal must meet WAC 173‑350 (solid waste) [DOE CSWD S‑1.1.1, p.588].
- Transportation of biosolids/sewage sludge: prepare a spill prevention/response plan; ensure vehicle cleaning; comply with WUTC rules [WAC 173‑308‑100, p.12].

6) Compare “belt” thickening vs drying beds for a 1,000‑person plant
- Footprint:
  - Drying beds (open): sizing guidance by sludge type; for primary+activated sludge, 1.75–2.50 ft²/capita (covered: 1.25–1.5 ft²/capita) [DOE CSWD Table S‑3, p.605]. For 1,000 people, that is ~1,750–2,500 ft² (open) or ~1,250–1,500 ft² (covered).
  - Wetter biosolids (e.g., aerobic digestion, impermeable beds, low net evaporation) require more area [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.6.C.1, p.605].
  - Mechanical belt units are equipment-based (compact) and, through thickening, reduce sizes of downstream storage, pumping, and piping [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.A, p.592], but verify with vendor layouts.
- Cost:
  - Ecology notes substantial cost differences among systems; perform a comparative cost analysis of all acceptable thickening systems as part of planning [DOE CSWD S‑2.1 intro, p.590–592].
- Operator attention:
  - Drying beds require frequent O&M: sand scarifying, controlled filling (initial ~8 in; normal depth ≈ 2× three‑day drawdown), raking/mixing crust, manual or equipment removal, makeup sand, and constant attention to drains and return flows [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.6.D, p.607].
  - Belt units require chemical preparation/dosing, adjustment of belt speed/tension, routine wash water and housekeeping, air/odor control, and routine O&M (see “mixing/chemical feeding,” “air handling/odor control,” “O&M” in S‑2.1.3 and S‑2.1.4) [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.3/S‑2.1.4 headings, p.587]. Belt processes use polymers [DOE CSWD p.605].
- Climate suitability:
  - Drying beds’ efficiency is heavily influenced by winter weather and rainfall; covered beds are recommended in western Washington; freezing/thawing and rain effects are documented [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.6.C.8, p.607].
  - Use covered beds and/or consider mechanical options where low net evaporation occurs [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.6.C.1 and C.8, p.605, p.607].
- Return flows:
  - Drying bed drainage returns to the plant; must be accounted for in design [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.6.C.6, p.605–607].
  - Belt thickening return flows should be similarly planned and monitored (see Step 5).

Washington note (drying beds and groundwater): If percolation or storage creates a “potential to contaminate,” a hydrogeologic study/monitoring plan under Ground Water Quality Standards may be required (e.g., discharges > agronomic rates or stored in impoundments) [DOE CSWD E3‑4, p.585].

7) Permits, plan review, and compliance setup
- WA facility planning and approvals:
  - Update General Sewer Plan and Engineering Report to include the selected thickening system and residuals management; submit Plans & Specs for Ecology approval before construction [DOE CSWD G1‑2.2, p.33; G1‑3.1/3.2, p.41].
  - Coordinate early with Ecology’s Regional Biosolids Coordinator; solids management will be reviewed by Water Quality Program with Solid Waste consultation [DOE CSWD S‑1.2.2 and S‑1.2.3, p.588–590].
- Biosolids permitting (WA):
  - Obtain/modify coverage under the Biosolids General Permit; the state program applies to all treatment works treating domestic sewage (including lagoons, incineration senders, disposers) [DOE CSWD S‑1.2.2, p.588–590].
  - Ensure compliance by the “person who prepares biosolids” [WAC 173‑308‑090, p.12], “applies” [WAC 173‑308‑110, p.12], and “transports” (spill plan, cleaning) [WAC 173‑308‑100, p.12].
  - Set up annual reporting to Ecology by March 1 [WAC 173‑308‑295, p.37].
- Federal Part 503:
  - Determine biosolids category (EQ/PC/CPLR/APLR) and set up required recordkeeping (pollutants, pathogen reduction certification/description, vector attraction reduction, management/site restrictions, site/location/amounts for CPLR) [EPA Guide, Recordkeeping/Reporting, p.58].
  - Use required analytical methods for biosolids samples [EPA Guide, p.154].
  - Reporting to EPA under Part 503 applies only if ≥1 mgd, ≥10,000 people, or Class I; a 1,000‑person plant typically is below the federal reporting threshold, but must still comply with Part 503 standards [EPA Guide, Who Must Report, p.26].

8) Testing, selection, and preliminary design
- Conduct bench/pilot tests of the shortlisted “belt” options (GBT, BFP w/ drum screen) using site sludge to establish achievable product % solids, capture, polymer dose, odor controls, wash water needs, and side-stream loads [DOE CSWD S‑2.1 intro, p.590–592].
- Compare alternatives on:
  - Ability to meet end‑use targets (thickening for digestion vs dewatering for disposal/use) and compliance (pathogen/VAR achieved by downstream processes) [DOE CSWD S‑1.2.1, p.589–590; WAC 173‑308‑270 referenced via WAC 173‑308‑290 form elements, p.37].
  - Capital/O&M costs and constructability [DOE CSWD S‑2.1 intro, p.590–592].
  - Footprint vs available space (cf. drying bed area per Table S‑3) [DOE CSWD p.605].
  - Operator capacity and safety (chemical handling; odor/air control per S‑2.1.3/S‑2.1.4) [DOE CSWD p.587].
  - Side‑stream impacts (return flows; monitoring plan) [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.6.C.6, p.605–607].
- Prepare an Engineering Report section with the selected alternative, preliminary design basis, and cost for Ecology review [DOE CSWD G1‑2.2, p.33; G1‑3.1/3.2, p.41].

9) Site selection, public involvement, and environmental review
- Develop a Site Selection Work Plan (if a new building/site is needed): define evaluation criteria (environmental, technical, institutional, cost), area needs, screening and detailed assessment, and public participation [WEF MOP8, Site Selection Work Plan, p.24].
- For Ecology‑funded projects, complete SERP/SEPA environmental review consistent with NEPA, including alternatives analysis and public participation [DOE CSWD G1‑2.7/2.8, p.41].

Washington-specific quick notes
- Contact Ecology early; the state biosolids rule is more stringent than federal and the permit process can take time [DOE CSWD S‑1.2.2, p.588–590].
- Screenings/grit/scum are managed under WAC 173‑350 (solid waste) [DOE CSWD S‑1.1.1, p.588].
- Drying beds in western WA: consider covered beds; plan for weather and return flows [DOE CSWD S‑2.1.6.C.8 and C.6, p.607, p.605–607].
- If land application: implement site management and access restrictions per WAC 173‑308; maintain certifications/records (e.g., pathogen/VAR and site restrictions) consistent with Part 503/WAC [EPA Guide, p.58; WAC 173‑308‑290 form elements, p.37].

General U.S. applicability quick notes
- Part 503 applies nationally; use EPA‑specified analytical methods [EPA Guide, p.154] and maintain recordkeeping appropriate to biosolids category [EPA Guide, p.58]. Reporting to EPA is limited to larger/Class I TWTDS [EPA Guide, p.26].
- EPA and WEF can provide technical assistance on beneficial use and biosolids management technology issues [EPA Guide, Assistance/Contacts, p.27–28].

Deliverables checklist for the planning phase
- Data package: current/future solids flows, % solids, return flows (sampling plan using EPA methods) [EPA Guide, p.154].
- Alternatives memo: GBT vs BFP (with drum screen) vs drying beds and other feasible options; bench/pilot plan and results; footprint comparison (beds per Table S‑3) [DOE CSWD p.605; p.587].
- Residuals/side‑streams plan: screenings (WAC 173‑350), filtrate routing/sampling, transportation spill plan [DOE CSWD p.588; WAC 173‑308‑100, p.12; DOE CSWD p.605–607].
- Compliance plan: end‑use pathway, pathogen/VAR strategy (downstream stabilization), Part 503/WAC recordkeeping/reporting setup, WA annual report [DOE CSWD S‑1.2.1, p.589–590; EPA Guide, p.58; WAC 173‑308‑295, p.37].
- Ecology submittals: General Sewer Plan/Engineering Report updates and Plans & Specs per G1; coordination with Biosolids Coordinator [DOE CSWD G1‑2.2, p.33; G1‑3.1/3.2, p.41; DOE CSWD S‑1.2.2/1.2.3, p.588–590].
- Public/stakeholder plan: site selection work plan and public participation [WEF MOP8, p.24].

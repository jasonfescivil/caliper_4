assistant: Lagoon feasibility analysis for small Washington wastewater systems

Summary of applicability and limits
- Stabilization ponds (facultative) and aerated lagoons are widely used by small communities as low‑cost, low‑technology wastewater treatment, particularly where land is available and effluent limits can be met. Long detention times aid pathogen removal, which is beneficial near shellfish harvest areas (Ecology Orange Book G3‑3.5; Lagoon & Liner Design Guidelines p.7) [2], [3], [10].
- Common limitations: variable suspended solids removal and short‑circuiting unless modified (e.g., multiple cells in series or baffles). Many lagoon systems cannot meet ammonia limits required for discharges to many freshwater streams, which can be a feasibility constraint (Lagoon & Liner Design Guidelines p.7) [10].

Lagoon types and process selection
- Facultative (waste stabilization ponds): Principal secondary treatment process for small systems; eligible for EPA TSS adjustment under 40 CFR 133.103(c) when criteria are met (EPA §133.103(c); §133.105(a)(1)) [4]. Identified in Ecology’s Criteria as “Ponds and Aerated Lagoons” (G3‑3.5) [3].
- Aerated lagoons: May be used to increase treatment; performance can be enhanced with mixing, baffling, multiple cells in series, mechanical aeration, and effluent filtration/polishing where needed (Ecology Orange Book—Small Systems supplemental; Lagoon & Liner Design Guidelines p.7; T7‑5) [9], [10], [5].

Siting and key constraints
- Liners (new impoundments): Ecology always requires geomembrane liners for newly constructed wastewater impoundments; existing unlined ponds are generally left as‑is unless integrity is disturbed or groundwater impacts occur (Lagoon & Liner Design Guidelines p.7) [10], [2].
- Groundwater protection and monitoring: Demonstrate compliance with Chapter 173‑200 WAC groundwater standards; Ecology sets enforcement limits during permitting. Generally, groundwater monitoring is not required if a double liner with leak detection is installed; single geomembrane liners require up‑ and down‑gradient groundwater monitoring wells (Orange Book—approval and monitoring; Chapter 173‑200 WAC) [1].
- Liner details and wind: PVC liners require a 12‑inch soil cover; in high‑wind areas riprap may be necessary. Follow manufacturer guidance on liner service life and UV exposure (Orange Book—liner notes) [1].
- Geohydrologic evaluation (land application/seepage lagoons): For land application systems (including seepage lagoons), submit a geohydrologic evaluation addressing depth to groundwater, seasonal movement, a water balance, and cumulative groundwater effects (WAC 173‑240‑140 plan elements) [13].
- Dam safety and embankments: Embankments must withstand the 100‑year flood event. Ecology’s Dam Safety Unit may exempt embankments ≤6 feet with minimal failure consequences; obtain written exemption from Ecology HQ. For multi‑cell systems, total impounded volume across cells generally governs dam safety applicability (Orange Book Ch. G1; Dam Safety) [7].
- Site/soil/seasonal conditions: Plan liner installation considering soil type, depth to groundwater, and seasonal temperature conditions (Orange Book—constraints) [7].
- Wind fetch and ice: High wind considerations noted above; specific ice design criteria are not provided in the excerpts (unknown) [1].

Hydraulic sizing and storage
- Non‑overflow (evaporative) lagoons: Size using a month‑by‑month hydraulic balance of wastewater volume, precipitation, and local evaporation. Use a “wet” year (1‑in‑10) design case (Orange Book—Impoundment Volume) [20].
- Waste treatment lagoons: Typically sized using complete‑mix, first‑order BOD kinetics: S/S0 = 1/(1 + k t); determine reaction coefficient k experimentally (Orange Book—Impoundment Volume) [20].
- Storage for land application: Land application systems often require winter storage to support nitrogen removal. Calculate required storage with a monthly hydraulic balance (wastewater, precipitation, evaporation, permitted discharges) using data from WSU AgWeatherNet, NCDC, or the WA Irrigation Guide Appendix B (Orange Book—Small Systems supplemental) [9].
- Hydraulics details: Pumps are typically sized for design flow with 1–5 ft residual head at the orifice (Orange Book—Small Systems) [2]. To reduce short‑circuiting, use multiple cells in series or baffles (Lagoon & Liner Design Guidelines p.7) [10].

Design loading and expected performance (BOD and TSS)
- Design flows and organic loadings are addressed in the Wastewater Treatment Technologies chapter (G3‑3), with lagoons in G3‑3.5 (Ecology Criteria) [3].
- BOD: Use first‑order complete‑mix kinetics as noted above; k must be justified with data (Orange Book—Impoundment Volume) [20].
- TSS: Pond and lagoon effluent TSS can be difficult to control consistently (Lagoon & Liner Design Guidelines p.7; Small Systems supplemental) [10], [2]. EPA allows state‑approved TSS adjustments for facilities that use waste stabilization ponds as the principal secondary treatment process and cannot achieve equivalent‑to‑secondary TSS, up to the maximum concentration demonstrated to be achieved 90% of the time in the state; the BOD5 30‑day average benchmark is 45 mg/L per §133.105(a)(1) (EPA §133.103(c); 49 FR 37005) [4].

Liner system selection, leak detection, and CQA/CQC
- Double‑liner systems: Establish an Action Leakage Rate (ALR) for the primary liner; continuously remove leakage to minimize head on the secondary geomembrane. Consider a conductive layer under the primary liner, perform liner integrity surveys (secondary liner/sump), and include ALR and response actions in the Engineering Report. Ecology is likely to set an ALR in the operating permit (Lagoon Double‑Liner Guidance; T7‑5.7.6 leak response) [6], [5].
- Single‑liner systems: Require electrical leak location testing capable of detecting 3‑mm holes (longest dimension) before service; propose equivalent post‑construction testing if applicable (Orange Book—constraints) [7].
- Construction QA/QC: Follow CQA/CQC practices for subgrade prep, liner specs, geomembrane stress cracking considerations, leak detection design, and access provisions (Lagoon & Liner Design Guidelines T7‑5.6 through T7‑5.10) [5].

Operations, monitoring, and residuals management
- O&M and performance: Operation, maintenance, and performance considerations are identified in G3‑3.3.9 and G3‑3.3.10 (Ecology Criteria) [3]. Routine inspections and permit‑specified monitoring typically include sampling by boat/raft at lagoon corners (Lagoon Double‑Liner Guidance) [6].
- Sludge/biosolids: Sludge in lagoons is “stored biosolids or sewage sludge” and must meet the state biosolids rule. A lagoon is “closed” when it receives no new material. After closure, solids may be left in place, removed, or managed onsite; requirements vary. Leaving biosolids/sludge in a closed lagoon >2 years without approval is considered surface disposal (solid waste) rather than biosolids (Orange Book—Closure and Biosolids) [15].
- Odor control reference: Orange Book cites WEF Manual of Practice No. 22 (1995) on odor control for small systems (G3—references) [8]. For general terminology/abbreviations, Orange Book references WEF MOP 11 (Vol. 3) (General—acronyms) [14].
- Monitoring methods (related to land application): Analytical methods are per 40 CFR Part 136; some immediate analyses may be done onsite. Monitoring frequency is set case‑by‑case and may be reduced if no adverse trends are evident (EPA/Orange Book—land treatment operations) [19].

Permitting and regulatory pathway
- Ecology approval: The owner/operator must submit the proposed system and obtain Ecology approval prior to installation (Orange Book—approval) [1]. Ecology reviews to ensure designs, construction, O&M, and effluent limits meet permit terms protecting public health and the environment (Orange Book—Permitting and Standards Context) [12].
- Water quality standards: Surface waters—Chapter 173‑201A WAC; groundwater—Chapter 173‑200 WAC. Ecology sets groundwater enforcement limits during permitting (Ecology Criteria reference; Orange Book—groundwater limits) [3], [1].
- Discharge permits and reclaimed water: Ecology relies on RCW 90.48, 90.52, 90.54; reclaimed water projects must follow RCW 90.46 (Orange Book—Permitting and Standards Context) [12].
- Groundwater monitoring vs liners: Double liner with leak detection generally avoids groundwater monitoring; single liners require up‑ and down‑gradient wells (Orange Book—monitoring) [1].
- Dam Safety: Obtain written exemption when applicable; design embankments for 100‑year flood (Orange Book Ch. G1) [7].
- Land application/seepage lagoons: Include geohydrologic evaluation and sound engineering justification that effluent/pretreatment will meet applicable permit limits (WAC 173‑240‑140) [13].
- Federal/EPA elements: EPA listed under coordinating federal agencies. EPA’s TSS adjustment policy for waste stabilization ponds may be applicable to WA permits per 40 CFR 133.103(c) (Orange Book—Permitting Coordination; EPA §133.103(c)) [12], [4].

Cost estimating and funding
- Cost requirements: Include both capital and O&M costs over project life; present total annual cost and present worth. Provide the engineer’s best opinion of probable final cost, methods used, and expected accuracy. At the engineering report stage, accuracy should meet AACE Class 3–4 (about −30% to +50%). Include construction, land acquisition, engineering (design/construction), permits, and all other foreseeable soft costs. Describe value engineering; include present‑worth O&M and a rough O&M category breakdown for alternative ranking (Orange Book—Cost Estimation Requirements) [11].
- Funding: Ecology’s Criteria lists potential Grant and Loan Programs (Ecology, CTED, USDA/RD) and the Small Town Environment Program (Ecology Criteria—Grant and Loan Programs) [3].

Feasibility checkpoints for small WA systems (apply AKART)
- Site feasibility: Adequate land; ability to meet WAC 173‑201A/173‑200; dam safety feasibility; liner constructability given soils/groundwater/season; wind protection for liners (Ecology Criteria G3‑3.5; Orange Book constraints) [3], [7], [1].
- Process feasibility: Meeting BOD/TSS limits with lagoon(s), recognizing potential need for modifications (series/baffles/aeration/filtration) and potential inability to meet ammonia limits for freshwater discharges (Lagoon & Liner Design Guidelines p.7; Small Systems supplemental) [10], [9].
- Storage feasibility (if land application): Demonstrated monthly balance and winter storage capacity using accepted climate data sources (Orange Book—Small Systems supplemental) [9].
- Permitting feasibility: Clear plan for liner system and leak detection (including ALR), groundwater compliance, and—if applicable—eligibility for EPA TSS adjustment for ponds (Orange Book; T7‑5; EPA §133.103(c)) [6], [5], [4].

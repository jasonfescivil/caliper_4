Short answer
- Ecology’s method: Set firm capacity at or above the peak hourly design flow, and size/number pumps so the station still delivers that peak with the largest pump out of service. Derive the peak from projected loads (20‑year+ horizon), I/I, and a peaking factor. Verify hydraulics over the full operating range and size the wet well to avoid short-cycling. [1][6]
- WEF guidance: In the provided record, WEF materials are cited only as general references; no specific, differing method is presented. Ecology/WAC allow use of WEF/WPCF Manuals as additional references, but the controlling standard for state approval is reasonable consistency with Ecology’s Criteria. [10][2]

How to design firm capacity per Ecology’s Criteria for Sewage Works Design (CSWD)
1) Establish the design peak
- Basis and horizon: “The firm capacity of a pump station shall be equal to or greater than the peak hourly design flow… recommended that the peak design flow be based on a 20‑year forecast or greater.” (C2‑1.2.1) [1]
- Include low flows: “review minimum flows and determine how the station will operate under low flow conditions.” (C2‑1.2.1) [1]
- Derive peak from sewers planning: “sewers shall be designed to carry at least the peak hourly flow… peaking factor… shall not be less than 2.5.” (C1‑3.3.2) [6] Include I/I as appropriate. [6]

2) Define firm capacity and redundancy
- Core rule: “firm capacity… equal to or greater than the peak hourly design flow.” (C2‑1.2.1) [1]
- Redundancy sizing: “The number of pumps… shall allow the station to provide the peak design flow with the largest pump out of order.” (C2‑1.2.3) [1]
- Starts/cycling: “number of pumps should correlate to the wetwell size and prevent excessively short periods between pump starts.” (C2‑1.2.3) [1]

3) Verify hydraulics across operating envelope
- Range check: “designed to operate under the full range of projected system hydraulic conditions… highest head and lowest head pumping conditions.” (C2‑1.2.2) [1]
- Pump selection/force main: choose a main “small enough… to minimize solids deposition yet large enough that the total head permits a good pump selection.” (C2‑1.2.2) [1]
- Reliability margin: “Selection of head loss coefficients… should be conservative.” (C2‑1.2.2) [1]
- Operability: “prevent a pump from operating for long periods… beyond the… normal operating range.” (C2‑1.2.2) [1]

4) Site and resilience criteria that affect firm capacity realization
- Flood: “designed to remain fully operational during the 100‑year flood/wave action.” (C2‑1.1.2) [1]
- Access, fire, utilities: provide access; “Conform to… NFPA 820.” (C2‑1.1.3 to C2‑1.1.5) [1]
- Piping layout/velocities/backflow and flexible connections. (C2‑1.1.5) [1]

Minimal step-by-step recipe you can follow in Washington
- Compute design average flow and add I/I per local data. (C1‑3.3.1, C1‑3.3.3) [6]
- Apply peaking factor (≥2.5 or local curve) to get peak hourly design flow. (C1‑3.3.2) [6]
- Set station firm capacity ≥ that peak. (C2‑1.2.1) [1]
- Choose number/size of pumps so peak is met with the largest pump out. (C2‑1.2.3) [1]
- Size wet well to avoid short cycles. (C2‑1.2.3) [1]
- Check full‑range hydraulics, select force main to control deposition and allow good pump selection; keep pumps within normal operating range. (C2‑1.2.2) [1]
- Provide 100‑year flood operability, access, fire protection (NFPA 820), and required site piping features (velocities, backflow protection). (C2‑1.1.2 to C2‑1.1.6) [1]
- Prepare submittals consistent with CSWD for Ecology review under WAC 173‑240‑040; use WEF/WPCF materials as supplemental references if needed. [10][2]

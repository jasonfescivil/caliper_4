A. Data sources and governance (put in Engineering Report intro)
- Primary population source: Washington Office of Financial Management (OFM) small-area population data (cite source and vintage). WAC 173‑240‑060 requires at least one year of current flow/loading data and population projections consistent with local planning; Ecology explicitly asks for OFM or other population data and consistency with local land-use agencies [WAC 173‑240‑060(3)(b); Ecology guidance p.48–49; “Provide OFM or other population data… projections… consistent with local land use planning agencies”] [2, p.48–49].
- Planning consistency: reference approved general sewer plan; if out of date, consult Ecology on which §060 items must be included in the engineering report [Ecology guidance to WAC 173‑240‑060] [2, p.48].
- Report completeness: ensure “sufficiently complete” so other engineers can prepare construction drawings without substantial changes [WAC 173‑240‑060(2); Ecology guidance] [2, p.48].

B. Residential base (EDUs)
- Dwelling units (DUs): list current and projected DUs by subarea; persons per DU from local planning/zoning densities (e.g., ranges by DU/acre) and planning staff concurrence [WEF FD‑5 §3.3 and §3.5 discussion of DU/acre and persons/DU; use most current planning documents] [18, p.55; 12, p.57].
- Vacancy factor: apply current local vacancy rate to housing stock before deriving occupied units/persons [WEF FD‑5 notes vacancy adjustment] [18, p.55].
- Per‑capita unit flow for design: for new systems use 100 gpd per person (DOE Orange Book Table G2‑2 “Dwellings,” per person) [G2‑1.2.4; Table G2‑2] [1, p.96]. Deviations must be substantiated in the engineering report with data (metering, billing, etc.) [G2‑1.2.4] [1, p.96].
- EDU definition (project-specific): 1 EDU = [X] persons/DU × 100 gpd/person. Example: 2.5 persons/DU → 250 gpd/EDU (DOE basis) [Table G2‑2] [1, p.96]. Note: If using metered data indicating different indoor use, document and justify per Ecology’s “credible data” requirement [Ecology guidance to WAC 173‑240‑060] [2, p.49].
- Note on conservation: future estimates must consider impacts of water conservation (higher waste load concentrations; per-capita flow assumptions must be addressed explicitly) [Ecology guidance to WAC 173‑240‑060] [8, p.49]; also avoid outdated unit factors if better local data exist [WEF FD‑5 cautions] [37, p.60].

C. Seasonal population and transients
- Identify seasonal‑use facilities (campgrounds, resorts) and transients (hotels/motels, group quarters). Convert to equivalent full‑time residents using appropriate multipliers or include them under institutional/commercial loads (see institutional factors below) [WEF FD‑5 §3.3 example and guidance] [18, p.55].
- For motels/hotels, if rooms‑only, DOE Table G2‑2 provides 130 gpd/room (at 65 gal/person) [Table G2‑2] [1, p.96].
- Address seasonal flow variation and consider flow equalization or modular capacity if variation is significant (small systems are flow‑sensitive) [WEF FD‑16 §5.3.1] [3, p.99].

D. Institutional and commercial loads (Population Equivalents and/or direct flow)
- Schools, restaurants, motels, trailer courts, etc.: use DOE Table G2‑2 unit flows and loadings for design (flow gpd/unit; BOD/TSS lb/d; flow duration hours). Example values:
  - Schools with showers/cafeteria: 16 gpd/person; 0.04 lb/d BOD; 8 hr [Table G2‑2] [1, p.96].
  - Restaurants: 50 gpd/seat; 0.2 lb/d BOD; 16 hr [Table G2‑2] [1, p.96].
  - Motels (rooms only): 130 gpd/room; 0.26 lb/d BOD; 24 hr [Table G2‑2] [1, p.96].
- Optionally compute “partial population equivalents” for employment/schools, increasing linearly with residential population (document method) [example approach shown in PPWTF study] [7, p.15 (Table 2‑1)].
- For employee‑based commercial uses (non‑process), forecast employees via land‑use plans and apply unit flows; best data are recent metered wastewater flows if available [WEF FD‑5 §3.4 and §3.5] [42, p.56; 12, p.57].

E. Infiltration/Inflow (I/I), wet weather, recycles
- Existing systems: base design on operating records; submit an Ecology‑acceptable plan to eliminate/handle excessive I/I so no discharge of inadequately treated wastewater occurs [G2‑1.2.3] [1, p.96].
- New/existing: design basis must consider wet‑weather flows, peak durations affecting detention, recirculation/plant recycle flows; select controlling condition (average vs. peak) [Design Basis narrative preceding Table G2‑2] [1, p.96].
- Describe current I/I, diurnal/seasonal variability; rely on calibrated meters (document calibration history) [WAC 173‑240‑060(3)(c) analysis; Ecology guidance] [2, p.48–49].
- Future estimates must account for climate‑change impacts on unmitigated I/I [Ecology guidance] [8, p.49].
- Collection system design requires “maximum exclusion of I/I; no new combined sewers” [C1‑1.3 DESIGN] [5, p.203].

F. Uncertainty bands and scenarios
- Provide low/medium/high (or multiple planning) scenarios based on alternative growth patterns, housing densities, or persons/DU; this is a recommended way to address forecast uncertainty [WEF FD‑5 §3.10] [11, p.75].
- Document triggers for moving from “medium” to “high” scenario (e.g., connection milestones) and how modular or equalization measures address variability [WEF FD‑16 §5.3.1] [3, p.99].

G. Reconciliation of data sets (billing vs. census vs. meters) — method to include in WAC 173‑240‑060(3)(c) section
- Assemble:
  - WWTP influent flow and load data (≥1 full year), from calibrated meters and accredited labs [WAC 173‑240‑060(3)(c); Ecology guidance on “credible and accurate data”] [2, p.48–49].
  - Water utility billing/meter data for sewered customers (note seasonal irrigation and other non‑return uses).
  - Latest OFM/census population and local planning DUs/persons/DU [2, p.48–49; 18, p.55].
- Steps:
  1) Compute return‑to‑sewer ratio by comparing billed indoor water use (or total less seasonal outdoor) to metered influent during dry‑weather periods; document assumptions (Ecology requires credible, calibrated data) [2, p.48–49].
  2) Reconcile occupied DU × persons/DU × unit flow against metered ADF; adjust persons/DU or unit flow only if justified by data (Ecology allows deviations with sound engineering judgment substantiated in the report) [G2‑1.2.4] [1, p.96].
  3) Cross‑check institutional/commercial contributions using Table G2‑2 vs. observed contributions (if submetered or known); adjust if justified [1, p.96; 12, p.57].
  4) Document diurnal/seasonal variation and identify RDII influence from wet‑weather periods [WAC 173‑240‑060(3)(c)] [2, p.48].
- Clearly state any discrepancies and the rationale for adopted design values, noting WEF’s caution against outdated factors and the preference for local measured data where credible [WEF FD‑5] [37, p.60; 12, p.57].

H. Design flow and load derivations (put calculations and summary tables here)
- Average Day Flow (ADF), current: sum of
  - Residential: Occupied population × 100 gpd/person (DOE basis) [Table G2‑2] [1, p.96].
  - Institutional/commercial: per Table G2‑2 (apply units: seats, rooms, persons, trailers; apply durations as needed) [1, p.96].
  - Plus recycles/plant returns (identify) [Design Basis narrative] [1, p.96].
- Future ADF (20‑yr): base on OFM/planning projections; account for conservation effects (higher concentrations; revisit per‑capita flow assumption) and climate‑impacted I/I [Ecology guidance] [8, p.49; 2, p.48–49].
- Peak design flows: use controlling condition per DOE—average or peak, whichever governs; consider peak duration impacts, wet weather, and recycles [Design Basis narrative] [1, p.96].
  - Apply appropriate peaking factors (reference DOE Criteria Figure C1‑1: Ratio of Peak Hourly Flow to Design Average Flow) and small‑system equalization considerations [DOE Criteria list; C1‑1 Figures] [5, List of Figures; p.203; 3, p.99].
- Organic loads: compute BOD/TSS using DOE Table G2‑2 per‑unit lb/day and same contributing populations/units; “design organic loading should be computed in the same manner used in determining design flow” [Design Basis narrative; Table G2‑2] [1, p.96].

Notes and small‑system cautions
- For new systems, DOE Table G2‑2 is the baseline. Deviations require sound engineering judgment and documentation in the report [G2‑1.2.4] [1, p.96].
- Even small systems must identify and address I/I; SWIS/small systems may need equalization/modularity for pronounced seasonal variations [3, p.99].
- Use credible data: Ecology requires Ecology‑accredited laboratory data and properly calibrated flow meters; include sampling locations, sample types, and return streams in the analysis [WAC 173‑240‑060(3)(c); Ecology guidance] [2, p.48–49].

assistant: Below is a concise, fill‑in template you can drop into small‑community (<1,000 pop.) engineering reports and general sewer plans. It aligns with WA Ecology submittal requirements and design-flow derivations.

A. Data sources and service area (maps and records)
- Service area maps: present and proposed sewer service boundaries; existing/proposed collection, pump stations/force mains; topography; nearby water bodies and discharge locations [20][38].
- Population data: “Provide Office of Financial Management (OFM) or other population data for the service area for at least the last two decades… Include population projections for the project planning area… for the project design period” [2].
- Current wastewater data: At least one full year of influent flow and loading; more than one year recommended; lab data from accredited labs; metering with documented calibration; identify sampling locations and return streams [2][8].

B. Baseline residential population and dwelling units
- Base year population (OFM): __________ [2]
- Design period (e.g., 20 years) population: low/most‑likely/high scenarios (see Uncertainty, Section H) [11][2].
- Dwellings and persons per dwelling (PPD):
  - Use official planning source for PPD: “Numbers of persons per dwelling should be based on planning projections derived from an official source” [1].
  - Note vacancy rate adjustment (include method and source); population/dwelling forecasts may be adjusted by housing vacancy [18].
- Seasonal population and transients:
  - Identify hotels/motels, campgrounds, seasonal homes; either convert to equivalent full‑time residents (EFr) using multipliers or handle as nonresidential/institutional sources below [18].

C. Nonresidential/institutional loading inventory (use unit factors)
List facilities and apply Table G2‑2 design bases for new systems [1]. Include flow duration to inform diurnal peaking.
- Schools with showers and cafeteria: 16 gpd/person; BOD 0.04 lb/d; SS 0.04 lb/d; 8 hr [1]
- Schools without showers (with cafeteria): 10 gpd/person; BOD/SS 0.025 lb/d; 8 hr [1]
- Boarding schools: 75 gpd/person; BOD/SS 0.2 lb/d; 16 hr [1]
- Motels (rooms only): 130 gpd/room; BOD/SS 0.26 lb/d; 24 hr [1]
- Trailer courts (3 persons/trailer): 300 gpd/trailer; BOD/SS 0.6 lb/d; 24 hr [1]
- Restaurants: 50 gpd/seat; BOD/SS 0.2 lb/d; 16 hr [1]
- Interstate restaurants, rest areas, service stations (use table entries as applicable) [1]
- Other commercial: use metered water use where available; “Where commercial buildings are to be included, the daily wastewater flows should be estimated individually… or metered water use” [3].

D. EDU definition and factors (document basis and alternatives)
- Preferred WA design basis for dwellings (new systems): 100 gpd per person; BOD/SS 0.2 lb/d per person [1].
  - Define 1 EDU flow = PPD × 100 gpd/person. Use official PPD source per [1]; justify any deviations with engineering report data [1][2].
- Alternative small‑system benchmarks for sensitivity checks:
  - 60 gpcd per capita (225 L/cap/d) where household size exceeds national median; household indoor 160–170 gpd (600–650 L/d) at 2.6 persons/household [3].
- Note conservation: “estimated future conditions should… account for increasing waste load concentrations and per capita flow rates attributable to water conservation” [8]; older unit factors may be high and lead to overdesign [37].

E. Design-flow derivations (average, peak, wet weather)
- Basis: “Process design… based on either the average design flow or the peak design flow, whichever is controlling” [1].
- Compute average annual day flow (AADF):
  - Residential AADF = (Residents × selected gpcd) or (EDUs × EDU gpd).
  - Nonresidential AADF = Σ(unit count × unit gpd) from Table G2‑2 or metered use [1][3].
- Organic loads: “The design organic loading should be computed in the same manner used in determining design flow” [1]. For residential, use 0.2 lb/d per person BOD and SS; add nonresidential per Table G2‑2 [1].
- Peak factors and diurnal duration:
  - Use observed diurnal patterns; consider “Peak flow rates continuing over a length of time sufficient to adversely affect the detention time of treatment units or the flow characteristics in conduits” [1].
  - For small systems with high variability, consider flow equalization and modular design to avoid oversizing for peaks [3].
- Wet‑weather/I&I and recycles:
  - Include “Wet weather flows” and “Recirculation and inplant recycle flows” in design [1].
  - “Sewer systems shall be designed… maximum exclusion of infiltration and inflow (I/I)” [5]; discuss I/I and proposed corrections in the engineering report [45].
  - Small systems are flow‑sensitive; identify/repair inflow sources and old building sewers, or provide capacity to handle excess water [3].
  - Future estimates should consider climate‑change impacts on unmitigated I/I [8].

F. Seasonal and transient adjustments
- If converting transients to EFr: document multipliers and seasonality window [18].
- Alternatively, keep as nonresidential loads using Table G2‑2 factors (e.g., motels, restaurants, rest areas) with appropriate flow durations [1].

G. Reconciliation of billing, census/OFM, and meters (QA/QC workflow)
- Step 1: Demographics. Compile OFM/census population and households; apply vacancy/PPD; map service area households [2][18][38].
- Step 2: Accounts. Tally active water and sewer customer accounts (EDUs). Compare to households and OFM population; document deltas (vacant dwellings, seasonal units).
- Step 3: Metered water use. Use production and customer meters (esp. commercial) to estimate wastewater where appropriate [3]. Flag non-sewered uses (e.g., irrigation) and significant nonreturn flows.
- Step 4: Wastewater meters. Compare to WWTP influent and key lift station meters; “credible and accurate data… meters with a documented history of proper calibration” [2][8].
- Step 5: Close gaps. Attribute differences to I/I (dry vs wet), recycles/returns, seasonal occupancy, and conservation. Adjust EDU factors or PPD only with substantiated data; deviations from Table G2‑2 must be “based on sound engineering judgment substantiated in the engineering report” [1].
- Step 6: Document variability. Provide at least one full year of flow/loading; more recommended; include diurnal/seasonal variation analysis and sampling/return stream locations [2].

H. Uncertainty bands and planning scenarios
- Acknowledge forecast uncertainty; use “Two or more planning scenarios” for population/land use [11].
- Provide uncertainty bands for:
  - PPD (range from official planning sources), vacancy, seasonal occupancy [18].
  - Unit flows (e.g., 100 gpcd per Orange Book vs 60 gpcd small‑system benchmark; conservation effects) [1][3][37][8].
  - I/I (dry vs wet year; climate‑driven wet‑weather range) [8][3].
- For small communities with greater variability, explicitly evaluate equalization/modularity to mitigate peaks [3].

I. Where each item goes in the submittal (map to required sections)
- General Sewer Plan (WAC 173‑240‑050) [20][47][38]
  - Purpose/need; ownership/O&M; service boundaries; layout maps [20].
  - Population trend and projections; methods and planning agency concurrence [20].
  - I/I problems and future actions [20].
  - Provisions for and adequacy of treatment (note conservation impacts and reclaimed water opportunities) [38].
  - Alternatives and selected strategy; costs per service; SEPA/NEPA statements [20][38].
- Engineering Report (WAC 173‑240‑060) [47][45][2][8]
  - (3)(b) Project description and service area maps [47].
  - (3)(c) Present and future quantity/quality (flows, BOD5, TSS), diurnal/seasonal variation, industrial/commercial characterization, I/I; at least one year of data; accredited labs; calibrated meters [2][8].
  - (3)(d) Degree of treatment; (3)(e) receiving water compliance [8].
  - (3)(g) Design data/sizing/expected efficiencies; include recycles/returns in hydraulics [45].
  - (3)(j) I/I, overflows/bypasses, proposed corrections [45].
  - Costs, staffing, future needs, SEPA/NEPA [45].
- Ecology design basis for new sewage works (Orange Book, Table G2‑2): apply per‑unit flows and loads above for nonresidential/institutional; dwellings per person at 100 gpd and 0.2 lb/d BOD/SS per person; use average or peak design flow “whichever is controlling” and include wet weather and recycle flows [1].

J. Small‑system notes to include
- For existing systems, base design on operating records and include an acceptable plan to eliminate/handle excessive I/I to avoid process impairment [1].
- “Variations in flows… from small populations can be significantly greater”; consider equalization and modular designs [3].
- Identify and repair/eliminate inflow sources in small systems, or design to handle excess water [3].

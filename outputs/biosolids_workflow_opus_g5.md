Workflow to evaluate biosolids options for a small (under 1,000) activated sludge system with land application in Washington State

1) Establish objectives, scope, and regulatory framework
- Define intended end use (beneficial land application vs. product such as compost/topsoil; disposal only as contingency) [1 S‑3 Residual Solids Management: End‑Use Options].
- Recognize biosolids are not solid waste if they meet standards; sludge that fails biosolids standards or is destined for landfill is solid waste and cannot be land-applied [7 WAC 173‑308‑060(1)–(4), p.5].
- Washington’s biosolids rule (WAC 173‑308) is more stringent than federal 40 CFR Part 503; Ecology operates a statewide 5‑year biosolids general permit—engage the regional biosolids coordinator early because permitting can be time‑consuming [10 S‑1.2.2 State (Rev. 08/2008)].
- Ensure compliance with related laws: water quality (chapter 90.48 RCW; chapters 173‑200 and 173‑201A WAC), SEPA for facilities, UTC rules for transport, and fertilizer laws if selling as fertilizer [2 WAC 173‑308‑030(1)–(4), p.3].

2) Quantify solids and plan stabilization appropriate for a small system
- For small communities/package systems, size and design stabilization so biosolids meet reuse/disposal objectives; develop accurate solids generation estimates to inform dewatering, transport, and end use; verify performance with data from similar systems [15 G3‑3.3.6 and G3‑3.3.10, p.188].
- Confirm overall goals include adequate stabilization to permit legal disposal or beneficial use [15 G3‑3.3.10 Performance, p.188].

3) Select treatment to meet pathogen reduction (Class A or B) and vector attraction reduction (VAR)
- Class A or B pathogen status and VAR are required under 40 CFR Part 503; enhanced digestion can produce Class A [10 S‑1.2.1 Federal].
- Choose a VAR pathway:
  - Option 1: ≥38% volatile solids reduction during treatment [4 EPA Guide, Option 1, p.131].
  - Option 3 (for aerobically digested biosolids typical of activated sludge plants): 30‑day bench test at 20°C shows <15% additional VS reduction for liquid biosolids (≤2% TS) [16 EPA Guide, Option 3, p.132].
  - Option 10: Incorporate into soil within 6 hours of application if using incorporation for VAR [12 EPA Guide, Option 10, p.134]. Note: Washington guidance also indicates mixing into dike/soil within 6 hours after application; if a VAR standard is already met, mixing is not mandatory [1 p.625; S‑4 References header page shows directive].

4) Evaluate end‑use options and hierarchy
- Preferred: beneficial use via land application directly or as component of compost/topsoil; state policy favors recycling and maximum beneficial use and discourages incineration and long‑term landfill reliance [1 S‑3 Residual Solids Management: End‑Use Options].
- If disposal (landfill) is needed on an emergency/short‑term basis, obtain approvals from the landfill, jurisdictional health department(s), and Ecology; meet applicable standards [1 S‑3 Residual Solids Management: End‑Use Options].

5) Determine pollutant limits pathway and product type
- Apply pollutant limit framework (e.g., PC/EQ under 503):
  - Pollutant Concentration (PC) option requires biosolids not exceed ceiling concentrations [14 EPA Guide, Option 2, p.44; see “ceiling concentration” definition tied to WAC 173‑308‑160 Table 1] [5 WAC 173‑308‑080 Definitions, “Ceiling concentration,” p.6].
  - Exceptional Quality (EQ) biosolids have no additional 503 restrictions unless set case‑by‑case by EPA or the state for bulk application [18 EPA Guide, For EQ Biosolids, p.54].
- Sampling and analytical methods must be representative and use approved methods (SW‑846/Part 136/503) [6 WAC 173‑308‑140(1)–(2), p.14].

6) Choose land application mode and agronomic rate approach
- Define agronomic rate method to supply N for target vegetation without violating groundwater/surface water standards [7 WAC 173‑308‑080 “Agronomic rate,” p.8].
- If using incorporation as VAR, plan operations to mix within 6 hours [12 EPA Guide, Option 10, p.134; 1 p.625].
- Plan to restrict public access as appropriate for site class and exposure [8 WAC 173‑308‑080 “Restrict public access,” p.10].

7) Permitting pathway and schedule
- All treatment works treating domestic sewage are under Ecology’s biosolids program; obtain coverage under the statewide biosolids general permit (5‑year) or an individual permit [10 S‑1.2.2 State (Rev. 08/2008)].
- File a Notice of Intent (NOI) for general permit coverage including required elements (permit name, activities, facility info, locations of treatment/storage/disposal/application sites, existing permits) [3 WAC 173‑308‑90002 Appendix 2, items (1)–(6), p.56].
- Coordinate SEPA for facilities as needed; SEPA public notice can be coordinated with WAC 173‑308 processes [2 WAC 173‑308‑030(3), p.3].

8) Prepare a Site Specific Land Application Plan (SS-LAP)
Include, at minimum:
- History of any prior biosolids with >Table 3 pollutant values at the site; crops and end uses; agronomic rate determination method [3 WAC 173‑308‑90003 Appendix 3, items (1)–(3), p.56].
- One‑mile vicinity map and access; treatment/storage sites; monitoring data (biosolids, soil, surface/groundwater), well depths; NPDES/other relevant permits; use/disposal practices and contractors; annual quantities [3 WAC 173‑308‑90003 Appendix 3, items (6)–(11), p.56].
- Provide any additional information Ecology requires to assess practices [3 WAC 173‑308‑90003 Appendix 3(11), p.56].

9) Design storage/handling to support selected option
- Tanks/basins: use corrosion‑resistant internal equipment (PVC, PE, stainless steel); large tanks typically concrete; smaller tanks may be coated carbon steel; provide mixing (aeration/mechanical/recycle); size for required retention; provide OSHA‑compliant access portholes; include odor control [1 S‑2.4.2 Tanks and Basins—A, B.1–5].
- Bulk storage (onsite or at land application site): size for production, timing, moisture; include contingency capacity for cleanouts/emergencies; comply with biosolids regulations and local health requirements; prevent run‑on/runoff; collect and treat leachate; design to minimize odors and secure against public/animal access [1 S‑2.4.3 A; B.1–5].

10) Transportation readiness
- Clean vehicles before hauling food/feed/fiber; maintain a spill prevention/response plan with contacts and cleanup procedures; transport otherwise regulated by the UTC [13 WAC 173‑308‑100(1)–(3), p.12].
- If transferring to another facility, ensure proper approvals/permits for exporting/receiving facilities [6 p.14 (export/transfer requirements)].

11) Implement land application operations
- Apply at agronomic rate; implement access controls; manage odors [7 WAC 173‑308‑080 “Agronomic rate,” p.8; 8 “Restrict public access,” p.10; 1 S‑2.4.3 B.4].
- If using incorporation for VAR, mix within 6 hours of application [12 EPA Guide, Option 10, p.134; 1 p.625].
- Seed/plant with vegetation identified in the SS‑LAP as applicable [1 p.625].

12) Groundwater protection check (because effluent and/or biosolids are managed on land)
- Apply antidegradation principles; where there is a “potential to contaminate” (discharge to land and rates > agronomic or storage in impoundments), a hydrogeologic study and monitoring plan may be required under Ecology guidance, even if a general permit covers the discharge [11 E3‑4 Ground Water Quality Standards Checklist, p.585].

13) Monitoring, recordkeeping, and reporting
- Use WAC 173‑308‑140 methods; maintain pathogen status, VAR data, and pollutant concentration data as required for permit coverage [6 WAC 173‑308‑140, p.14; 20 EPA Guide, Application content, p.165].
- Maintain and submit site/application quantities and monitoring data per SS‑LAP and permit [3 WAC 173‑308‑90003 Appendix 3, items (7), (10), p.56].

14) Contingency planning
- Plan for emergency/short‑term landfill disposal only with required approvals; incineration and long‑term landfill reliance are discouraged [1 S‑3 Residual Solids Management: End‑Use Options].

15) Small‑system O&M considerations
- Provide adequate O&M space, mixing/air systems, and flexibility similar to built‑in‑place facilities; verify process performance with data from comparable plants; ensure aeration and solids handling match expected loads [15 G3‑3.3, incl. O&M and Performance, p.188].

Outcome: This workflow yields a permitted, defensible biosolids program that favors beneficial land application at agronomic rates with appropriate stabilization/VAR, storage, transport controls, and groundwater protection, consistent with WAC 173‑308 and Ecology’s design standards.

### Population Projection Methodology
- Population projections for small wastewater systems should be based on historical data from the Office of Financial Management (OFM) or other sources for the service area, covering at least the last two decades if available [7]. Projections must extend to the project design period and include the planning area and concentrated growth areas [7].
- Base projections on historical records from the approved general sewer plan, ensuring consistency with data from local land use planning agencies [7]. Briefly describe the method used (e.g., trend analysis) and obtain concurrence from applicable local or regional planning agencies [11].
- For new systems, numbers of persons per dwelling should be based on planning projections from official sources, with any deviations justified by sound engineering judgment in the engineering report [5].
- Specific methodology for Eastern Washington is unknown from the provided context; general methods above apply unless supplemented by local data.

### Design Flow Calculations
- **Average Daily Wastewater Flow (ADWF)**: For new systems, design ADWF based on per capita flows from Table G2-2 (e.g., 100 gpd per person for dwellings, 75 gpd per person for boarding schools) [5]. These figures assume normal infiltration; add allowances for unfavorable conditions like high groundwater [1]. If an existing water system exists, use water consumption figures to substantiate per capita flow [1]. Alternative methods (e.g., based on water consumption records or actual measured flows) may be used with documentation provided to Ecology for review and approval [1].
- **Peak Design Wastewater Flow (PDWF)**: Sewers should be designed to carry at least the peak hourly flow at capacity [1]. Calculate PDWF as design average daily flow multiplied by a peaking factor from Figure C1-1 (e.g., ?4.0 for 0.1 thousand population, decreasing to ?2.0 for 100 thousand) [1], with a minimum peaking factor of 2.5 [1]. Agencies may use local peaking factors based on actual flow data [1].
- **Peaking Factors**: Use the Ten States Standards equation PF = (18 + (square root)P)/(4 + (square root)P), where P is population in thousands [2]. This provides peaking factors as a function of population, though they may be higher than observed in modern systems [2]. Factors include I/I and decrease with increasing average flow [8]. For small systems, peaks may be higher due to smaller average flows [8].
- **Infiltration/Inflow (I/I) Allowances**: Per capita flows and peaking factors cover normal I/I for new systems built with modern techniques [1]. Make additional allowances for existing systems or unfavorable conditions (e.g., high groundwater, older systems, illicit connections) based on actual flow data [1]. For existing systems, provide a plan to eliminate or handle excessive I/I to avoid inadequate treatment or discharges, as required by WAC 173-240 [5]. I/I remains reasonably constant except during rainfall [8]; assess using flow measurements [1].

- All designs must comply with WAC 173-240, including submission of an engineering report with flow estimates (e.g., present and future quantity/quality of wastewater) [9], [11]. Use AADF, MMDF, MWDF, MDDF, PHDF, and PIDF as defined for hydraulic and biological capacity [6]. For small systems, justify safety factors to ensure permit compliance [6].

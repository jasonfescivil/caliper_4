# Workflow for Biosolids Options: Activated Sludge to Land Application in Washington State (Community <1000)

## Step 1: Understand Regulatory Framework
- Review Washington State biosolids rule (Chapter 173-308 WAC) and federal requirements (40 CFR Part 503) [10]
- Contact Ecology's Regional Biosolids Coordinator early in planning process [10]
- Recognize that biosolids are not solid waste when meeting quality standards [7]

## Step 2: Solids Treatment Planning

### A. Stabilization Options
- **Aerobic Digestion**: Consider for small systems; evaluate volatile solids reduction of at least 38% [4]
- **Anaerobic Digestion**: Evaluate for pathogen and vector attraction reduction [7]
- Consider enhanced digestion processes (thermophilic anaerobic, dual digestion, autothermal aerobic) for Class A biosolids production [10]

### B. Dewatering/Concentration
- Evaluate thickening methods: gravity thickening, dissolved air flotation, gravity belt filtration, belt filter press, or centrifuges [10]
- Conduct bench-scale or pilot testing when possible [10]

## Step 3: Biosolids Classification

### A. Pathogen Reduction
- Determine Class A or Class B pathogen reduction requirements [10]
- Class A requires one of six alternative processes [10]

### B. Vector Attraction Reduction
- Select appropriate option (e.g., 38% volatile solids reduction, incorporation within 6 hours, or other approved methods) [4][12]
- Note: If biosolids meet vector attraction reduction standard, mixing into dikes within 6 hours is not mandatory [1]

### C. Pollutant Limits
- Ensure ceiling concentrations in Table 1 (WAC 173-308-160) are not exceeded [5]
- Determine if biosolids qualify as Table 3 or lower pollutant concentration limits [3]

## Step 4: Storage Design
- Size storage based on biosolids quantity, timing of use, and moisture content [1]
- Provide additional space for scheduled cleaning and emergencies [1]

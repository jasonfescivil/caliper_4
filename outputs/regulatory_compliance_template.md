# Small Wastewater Treatment Plant Facility Plan Template

This template is designed to assist in the development of an Engineering Report for a domestic wastewater facility, as required by the Washington State Department of Ecology. The content aligns with the requirements outlined in the Washington Administrative Code (WAC), particularly WAC 173-240-060, and is tailored for systems serving populations of 0-2,000 people.

An Engineering Report is a key document in the required schedule of tasks for constructing or modifying domestic wastewater facilities, preceding the submission of plans and specifications [8, p.3; WAC 173-240-030(3)]. It must be sufficiently complete so that plans and specifications can be developed from it without substantial changes [3, p.6; WAC 173-240-060(2)].

## 1. Population and Flow Projections

This section establishes the basis of design for the facility by projecting future population and wastewater flows over the planning period.

### 1.1. Population Projections
- **Current Population:** State the current population within the existing and proposed service boundaries [1, p.1; WAC 173-240-050(3)(c)].
- **Projected Population:** Provide the estimated future population for the stated design period (e.g., 20 years). Describe the methodology used to determine future population trends and note the concurrence of any applicable local or regional planning agencies [3, p.6; WAC 173-240-050(3)(e)].

### 1.2. Design Flow Calculations
This section should state the present and expected future quantity of wastewater [3, p.6; WAC 173-240-060(3)(c)]. For small systems, estimates should be based on population projections rather than the number of buildings [24, p.99; 5.3.1].
- **Average Daily Wastewater Flow (ADWF):** Calculate the ADWF based on a per capita water usage rate. A typical value is 225 L/capita/d (60 gpd/capita) multiplied by the total projected population [24, p.99; 5.3.1].
- **Peak Flow Calculations:** Determine peak flow rates that could adversely affect the detention time of treatment units [18, p.94; General Considerations].
  - **Peaking Factors:** Note that flow variations (daily, weekly, seasonal) from small populations can be significantly greater than in large systems. Justify the selected peaking factor [24, p.99; 5.3.1]. If peak hour flow is at least three times the average dry weather flow, the design must include multiple parallel trains of unit processes [18, p.94; General Considerations].
  - **Infiltration and Inflow (I/I):** Include a discussion of any existing I/I problems and the actions that will be taken to alleviate them. I/I should not be overlooked in small systems, as they are highly flow-sensitive [3, p.6; WAC 173-240-050(3)(g)][24, p.99; 5.3.1].

### 1.3. Flow Measurement and Monitoring
Describe the proposed methods for flow measurement. Plans and specifications, which are developed from this report, must include schematics for flow measuring devices [4, p.15; WAC 173-240-140(2)(g)].

## 2. Treatment Process Selection

This section provides a detailed analysis of treatment alternatives and justifies the selected process, ensuring it represents "all known, available, and reasonable methods of prevention, control, and treatment" (AKART) [5, p.177; G3-1.1.1].

### 2.1. AKART and Alternatives Analysis
- **Treatment Alternatives:** Discuss the various treatment alternatives evaluated and provide the reasons they are unacceptable [3, p.6; WAC 173-240-060(3)(f)]. For small systems, technologies may include package treatment systems, recirculating gravel filters, ponds, aerated lagoons, land treatment systems, and constructed wetlands [5, p.177; Criteria for Sewage Works Design].
- **Process Selection:** Describe the type of treatment process proposed, based on the character of the wastewater, the method of disposal, and the degree of treatment required [3, p.6; WAC 173-240-060(3)(f)]. Provide sound engineering justification for the selected process through pilot plant data, results from similar installations, or scientific literature, demonstrating that the effluent will meet applicable permit limitations [4, p.15; WAC 173-240-130(q)].

### 2.2. Compliance with Design Standards
The facility design will be reviewed for consistency with the "Criteria for sewage works design" and other standard engineering references [2, p.4; WAC 173-240-040(2)]. While the "Ten States Standards" is a useful reference, it should not be viewed as containing absolute design values [12, p.8].

## 3. Effluent Standards and Permit Requirements

This section outlines the regulatory requirements the facility must meet. The Department of Ecology will review the engineering report to determine if the proposed facility will be designed, constructed, and operated to meet effluent limitations and other permit requirements [2, p.4; WAC 173-240-040(1)].

### 3.1. Effluent Standards
- **Surface Water Discharge:** Identify the degree of treatment required based on applicable permits, rules, and the receiving body of water [3, p.6; WAC 173-240-060(3)(d)]. The design must meet standards established in WAC 173-221 (Discharge Standards and Effluent Limitations) and WAC 173-201A (Water Quality Standards for Surface Waters of the State of Washington) [5, p.177; G3-1.1.1, G3-1.1.2].
- **Groundwater Discharge (if applicable):** If using subsurface disposal, the facility must comply with the restrictions in WAC 173-240-035. The report must include a geohydrologic evaluation of factors such as depth to groundwater and a water balance analysis [8, p.3; WAC 173-240-035][4, p.15; WAC 173-240-130(p)].

### 3.2. Permit Requirements
- **NPDES/State Waste Discharge Permit:** State the anticipated requirements of the National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) or state waste discharge permit [2, p.4; WAC 173-240-040(1)].
- **Monitoring and Reporting:** The permit will specify monitoring locations, frequency, and sample collection methods, as well as reporting requirements [11, p.194].
- **Environmental Review:** Include a statement regarding compliance with the State Environmental Policy Act (SEPA) and the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA), if applicable [3, p.6; WAC 173-240-060(n)].

### 3.3. Biosolids Management
- **Compliance with 40 CFR Part 503:** Discuss the method of final sludge disposal selected and any alternatives considered [4, p.15; WAC 173-240-130(r)]. All sewage sludge use and disposal practices must comply with 40 CFR Part 503 [11, p.194]. This part applies to any person who prepares or applies sewage sludge [15, p.2; § 503.2(b)].
- **Reporting:** Note that annual reporting requirements under 40 CFR 503.28 apply to POTWs with a design flow of 1 MGD or more, or those serving 10,000 people or more [23, p.21; § 503.28]. Facilities below these thresholds are not subject to this specific reporting requirement but must still meet all other applicable Part 503 standards.

## 4. Design Criteria and Standards

This section must include the basic design data and sizing calculations for each unit of the treatment works, along with expected efficiencies [3, p.6; WAC 173-240-060(3)(g)].

### 4.1. Hydraulic and Organic Loading Criteria
- **Hydraulic Design:** The hydraulic capacity of all pumping, conveyance channels, and pipes should be based on the peak instantaneous design flow (PIDF). The design of clarifiers should be based on the peak hourly design flow (PHDF) [18, p.94; Table G2-1].
- **Organic Loading:** The design of biological reactors should be based on the maximum week design load (MWDL) for secondary treatment or the maximum day design load (MDDL) for toxics removal. Aeration equipment design should be based on the peak hourly design load (PHDL) [18, p.94; Table G2-1].

### 4.2. Reliability and Redundancy
- **Safety Factor:** The design must include an appropriate safety factor to ensure compliance with permit limits and water quality standards under maximum flow and loading conditions [18, p.94; General Considerations].
- **Redundancy:** If the peak hour flow and solids loading are at least three times the average dry weather values, the design must include multiple parallel trains of unit processes [18, p.94; General Considerations]. For disinfection at reuse and reliability class I plants, the system must be able to treat all flows [18, p.94; Table G2-1].

## 5. Cost Estimates and Funding

This section details the financial plan for the project, a required component of the engineering report [6, p.1; Definitions].

### 5.1. Cost Projections
- **Capital Costs:** Provide an estimate of the total capital cost for the project.
- **Operation and Maintenance (O&M) Costs:** Project the annual O&M costs for the new facility.
- **Cost Per Service:** Include a discussion and table showing the cost per service in terms of both debt service and O&M costs during the planning period [3, p.6; WAC 173-240-050(l)].

### 5.2. Funding Plan
- **Funding Sources:** Describe the recommended financing method [9, p.2; Definitions]. Identify potential funding sources, such as grants and loans from the Department of Ecology, the Department of Community, Trade, and Economic Development (CTED), or the USDA/Rural Development (USDA/RD) [5, p.177; G3-2.2.2].
- **Funding Eligibility:** Note that entities should contact Ecology early in the project development phase for information on timelines and current funding eligibility requirements [17, p.41; G1-2.8].

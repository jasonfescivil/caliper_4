Workflow to determine and use 7Q10 (for a small Washington community)

1) Confirm the need to use 7Q10
- For chronic aquatic life criteria, the critical receiving-water flow is typically the 7Q10 low flow; if any applicable criterion could be exceeded under this critical condition, WQBELs must be developed [2010 EPA PWM, p.145 (Reasonable Potential Determination); 6-4.1.1 in text; [1]].

2) Define the receiving water and outfall context (planning documents)
- In your general sewer plan and engineering report, map and describe the receiving water(s): stream locations, flow directions, and high/low water surfaces near the outfall; identify existing/potential discharge locations [WAC 173-240-050(3)(d)(vi), [17]; WAC 173-240-060(3)(e), [20]].  
- Describe applicable water quality standards and how they will be met outside any dilution zone [WAC 173-240-060(3)(e), [20]].  
- Keep these submittals consistent with Ecology’s planning/approval sequence (general sewer plan → engineering report → plans/specs) [DOE Orange Book G1-2.2/G1-2.3, p.33; Figure G1-1, [2]; G1-3, p.41–45, [12][14]].

3) Acquire data and choose the method per agency practice
- Obtain the best available hydrologic record for the receiving stream sufficient to determine the low-flow statistic. Follow Ecology/permitting-authority policy or established past permitting practices for selecting critical flow values/methods to ensure consistency [2010 EPA PWM, Effluent Flow—analogy on following policy for critical flows, p.133, [18]].
- Note: EPA guidance indicates the chronic critical stream flow might be the 7Q10; select the appropriate low-flow metric per state policy for your waterbody/type [2010 EPA PWM, p.145, [1]].

4) Determine the 7Q10
- Using the method accepted by Ecology for your waterbody (as identified in Step 3), determine the 7Q10 (the 7‑day average low flow with a 10‑year recurrence). Document the dataset, period of record, calculation approach, and any assumptions in the engineering report [WAC 173-240-060(3)(e), [20]; plan/report mapping and receiving water details per [17][20]].

5) Select the critical effluent design flow
- Identify the critical effluent flow Qd for modeling per permitting authority policy or past practice (e.g., design flow, max monthly average, etc.) to ensure consistency [2010 EPA PWM, p.133, [18]].

6) Choose mixing assumptions and perform mass-balance
- Decide whether to assume rapid and complete mixing or incomplete mixing, then apply the appropriate mass-balance framework to project downstream concentrations using Qd and the 7Q10 receiving-water flow [2010 EPA PWM, Exhibits 6-13 to 6-15, p.6-26 to 6-29, [26]; narrative on acute vs chronic critical flows, p.145, [1]].

7) Make the reasonable potential and WQBEL determinations
- Compare the projected chronic-condition concentration (using 7Q10) with the chronic criteria. If there is reasonable potential to exceed any applicable criterion, develop WQBELs; repeat for all pollutants/criteria as needed [2010 EPA PWM, p.145, [1]].

8) Document transparently
- In the NPDES fact sheet, document how you determined and used 7Q10, the data and methods, applicable standards, antidegradation, and how WQBELs were derived [2010 EPA PWM, 6.4.1.5 Step 5, p.151, [5]].  
- Ensure the engineering report includes the receiving-water description, degree of treatment required, design basis, and how standards will be met outside any dilution zone [WAC 173-240-060(3)(d)–(g), [20]].

9) Small-community planning notes (<1,000 population)
- Maintain consistency between population/service-area assumptions in the general sewer plan and engineering report (maps, growth projections, and receiving-water mapping) even for small systems [DOE Orange Book G1-3.1 and G1-3.2, p.41–45, [12][14]; WAC 173-240-050(3)(e), [17]].  
- If only extending sewers within an approved plan, an engineering report may be waived; however, permitting analyses that rely on 7Q10 for WQBELs still apply where discharges occur [DOE Orange Book G1-4.1.2, p.45, [29]].

10) Environmental review linkage
- Complete SEPA (and, if applicable, NEPA/SERP) for plan/report actions; you can tier project-specific reviews from programmatic reviews in the general sewer plan and engineering report [DOE Orange Book G1-2.7, p.39–41, [9][12]].

Output: A documented 7Q10 value for the receiving water; its application in reasonable potential and WQBEL calculations; and traceable documentation in the engineering report and NPDES fact sheet per the cited requirements.

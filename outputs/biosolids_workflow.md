Workflow for evaluating biosolids options for a small (under 1,000) activated‑sludge system with land application in Washington State

1) Frame the project and engage regulators early
- Contact Ecology’s Regional Biosolids Coordinator early; WA’s biosolids rule (WAC 173‑308) is more stringent than federal, and Ecology implements a statewide 5‑year general permit for biosolids management covering all treatment works treating domestic sewage [DOE Orange Book S‑1.2.2, p.590‑591] [10].  
- Coordinate SEPA/public notice, and be aware of other applicable laws (e.g., groundwater and surface water standards; commercial fertilizer rules if selling product) [WAC 173‑308‑030, p.3(1),(3),(4)] [2].

2) Quantify solids and set end‑use objectives
- Estimate solids production and stabilization needs; accurate estimates are necessary to size dewatering, storage, transport, and end‑use options [DOE Orange Book G3‑3.3.6, p.188] [15].  
- Define performance targets including pathogen reduction and adequate stabilization to permit legal beneficial use [DOE Orange Book G3‑3.3.10, p.188] [15].  
- Prioritize recycling/beneficial use (land application directly or via compost/topsoil products); WA discourages incineration and long‑term landfill disposal, which require additional approvals if used even short‑term [DOE Orange Book S‑3, p.625] [1].

3) Select biosolids quality pathway (Class A vs Class B) and vector attraction reduction (VAR)
- Class A/B pathogen paths are established under 40 CFR Part 503; VAR is also required [DOE Orange Book S‑1.2.1, p.590] [10].  
- Options to meet VAR include:  
  - 38% volatile solids reduction during treatment (typical of aerobic/anaerobic digestion) [EPA Part 503 Guide, Option 1, p.131] [4];  
  - For liquid aerobically digested biosolids ≤2% TS, a 30‑day bench test showing <15% additional VS reduction [EPA Part 503 Guide, Option 3, p.132] [16];  
  - Incorporation into soil within 6 hours of application (mixing) [EPA Part 503 Guide, Option 10, p.134] [12].  
- Field practice note: if biosolids meet a VAR standard, post‑application mixing is not mandatory; otherwise, incorporate within 6 hours and promptly seed/plant per the Site Specific Land Application Plan [DOE Orange Book S‑4 refs/S‑2.4.2 note, p.625] [1].

4) Confirm pollutant limits and establish monitoring methods
- Ensure pollutant concentrations do not exceed ceiling concentrations; the Pollutant Concentration option requires meeting the ceiling limits [EPA Part 503 Guide, Option 2, p.44] [14]; see “ceiling concentration” definition [WAC 173‑308‑160/Table 1 via definition, p.6] [5].  
- Sampling must be representative; use WAC‑specified analytical methods (or 40 CFR 136/503) for metals and other parameters [WAC 173‑308‑140(1)‑(2), p.14] [6].

5) Permitting pathway
- Obtain coverage under Ecology’s Biosolids General Permit (5‑year). The Notice of Intent must include, at minimum: the permit sought, activities requiring coverage, facility identity and operator info, and locations where biosolids are treated/stored/applied (and any local solid waste permits) [DOE Orange Book S‑1.2.2, p.590‑591] [10]; [WAC 173‑308‑90002 Appendix 2, p.56] [3].  
- A person may not apply biosolids except per WAC 173‑308 and permit conditions [WAC 173‑308‑110, p.12] [13].  
- Relationship to other laws: facilities and sites must also comply with chapter 90.48 RCW and surface/groundwater standards (chapters 173‑201A and 173‑200 WAC) [WAC 173‑308‑030(4), p.3] [2].

6) Prepare the Site Specific Land Application Plan (LAPP)
- Include, at minimum:  
  - Site history for any prior biosolids with pollutants above WAC 173‑308‑160 Table 3 values (dates, amounts, pollutant levels, areas) [WAC 173‑308‑90003(1), p.56] [3];  
  - Crops and their end uses; current crop distribution [WAC 173‑308‑90003(2), p.56] [3];  
  - How agronomic rates will be determined (and any current calculations) [WAC 173‑308‑90003(3), p.56] [3]; “agronomic rate” is the N‑based rate that avoids violating groundwater/surface water standards [WAC 173‑308‑080, p.8] [7];  
  - 1‑mile map with access routes (and for associated storage/treatment) [WAC 173‑308‑90003(6), p.56] [3];  
  - Available biosolids, soils, and water monitoring data (with well depths if applicable) [WAC 173‑308‑90003(7), p.56] [3];  
  - Description of use/disposal practices and any transfer locations/contract applicators [WAC 173‑308‑90003(8), p.56] [3];  
  - Amounts produced/applied last year and projected annually [WAC 173‑308‑90003(10), p.56] [3].  
- Include pathogen and VAR data and pollutant concentrations as part of the application package [EPA Part 503 Guide, p.165(A)] [20].

7) Design storage, handling, and odor control appropriate for small systems
- Tanks/basins: use corrosion‑resistant internals; provide mixing (aeration, mechanical, or recycle); size for required detention; provide OSHA‑compliant access; plan for odor control since even short storage of unstabilized solids can cause nuisance odors [DOE Orange Book S‑2.4.2.B(1)‑(5), p.625] [1].  
- Bulk storage (at plant or site): size for production, timing, moisture; add capacity for scheduled cleanouts and emergencies [DOE Orange Book S‑2.4.3.B(1), p.625] [1]; comply with biosolids regs and local health requirements [S‑2.4.3.B(2), p.625] [1]; manage run‑on/runoff and collect/treat leachate [S‑2.4.3.B(3), p.625] [1]; design to minimize odor complaints (consider stability, population density, winds) [S‑2.4.3.B(4), p.625] [1]; secure against public/animal access [S‑2.4.3.B(5), p.625] [1].  
- For land application systems with potential to affect groundwater, follow WA antidegradation: assess potential to contaminate; if discharge exceeds agronomic rates or uses impoundments, a hydrogeologic study and monitoring plan may be required [DOE Orange Book E3‑4, p.585] [11].

8) Transportation logistics and spill preparedness
- Clean transport vehicles before hauling food/feed/fiber crops; maintain a spill prevention/response plan with contacts and cleanup methods; transport is also subject to the Utilities and Transportation Commission [WAC 173‑308‑100(1)‑(3), p.12; WAC 173‑308‑030(2), p.3] [13][2].

9) Application operations
- Apply at agronomic rate based on crop N needs [WAC 173‑308‑080 definition, p.8] [7];  
- If relying on incorporation for VAR, mix into soil within 6 hours of application [EPA Part 503 Guide, Option 10, p.134] [12]; if another VAR standard is met, incorporation is not mandatory [DOE Orange Book S‑4 refs note, p.625] [1];  
- Seed/plant per the LAPP after application [DOE Orange Book S‑4 refs note, p.625] [1];  
- Restrict public access as appropriate; “restrict public access” means minimizing access by nonessential personnel through barriers, signs, remoteness, etc. [WAC 173‑308‑080 definition, p.10] [8].

10) Monitoring, recordkeeping, and reporting
- Use required sampling/analytical methods; maintain representative sampling of applied biosolids [WAC 173‑308‑140(1)‑(2), p.14] [6].  
- Keep/submit data on pathogen/VAR status and pollutant concentrations, plus site mapping and well info as required by permitting documents [EPA Part 503 Guide, p.165(A)] [20]; [WAC 173‑308‑90003, p.56] [3].

11) Contingency and alternative pathways
- Provide extra storage capacity for emergencies (e.g., digester cleaning, weather) [DOE Orange Book S‑2.4.3.B(1), p.625] [1].  
- If disposal is necessary, note that landfilling or incineration is discouraged and requires additional approvals; sewage sludge disposed in a landfill is solid waste (not biosolids) and regulated under solid waste rules [DOE Orange Book S‑3, p.625] [1]; [WAC 173‑308‑060(4), p.5] [7].

12) Distribution of EQ products (if pursued)
- If biosolids meet Exceptional Quality criteria, distribution restrictions may be minimal unless set by the permitting authority [EPA Part 503 Guide, p.54] [18].  
- If the product meets the definition of a commercial fertilizer, comply with WA Dept. of Agriculture rules (chapter 15.54 RCW; chapter 16‑200 WAC) [WAC 173‑308‑030(1), p.3] [2].

This workflow aligns selection, permitting, design, and operations with WA’s biosolids rule, Part 503, and Ecology’s design standards for small communities prioritizing beneficial land application at agronomic rates, with appropriate quality controls, storage/odor management, groundwater protection, transport, and contingency planning.

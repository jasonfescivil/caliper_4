Since the Infiltration and Inflow Study was completed in 2006, the City of Tekoa’s sewage collection system has undergone several infiltration and inflow (I/I) reduction projects in the last 12 years. The infiltration and inflow coming into the system has been greatly reduced but there is still significant I/I that enters the collection system, generally in the spring of the year. This Infiltration and Inflow Analysis will examine the level of reduction of infiltration and inflow that has been accomplished in the last 12 years as well as what recommended improvements still need to be completed to further reduce I/I.

## EPA Guidance on Infiltration/Inflow
In 1985, the EPA provided a guidance document for Infiltration and Inflow analysis and project certification. In the document, the determination of non-excessive infiltration is identified as total flows of 120 gallons per capita per day (dry weather flow + I/I). It should be noted that after the guidance document was published, new laws were enacted for new construction to install low flow fixtures which has had an impact on dry weather sewage flows. For a community like Tekoa without significant development since the low flow fixture laws were enacted in 1992, the 120 gpcd is probably still appropriate. For the City of Tekoa, seasonal I/I plays a large part in the average daily flows in the sewer system. ## 2006 Infiltration and Inflow Study

The Infiltration and Inflow Study was completed in conjunction with the City of Tekoa’s Comprehensive Sewer Plan that analyzed the City’s sewage collection system. The Plan was completed in 2006 and approved by the Washington State Department of Ecology in November 2006. The data collected for the 2006 plan included monitoring data at fourteen different manhole locations. The data was collected non-concurrently over a period of three months using three flow monitoring units. With three monitors and fourteen collection locations, only three concurrent locations were monitored at a time. With the non-concurrent monitoring data, it was difficult to determine where I/I was coming from during seasonal variation. It was also difficult to determine the amount of I/I that each area was contributing during seasonal variation as found in the monitoring data at the treatment plant. An area that showed no rain dependent I/I during monitoring periods subsequently was found to be a high I/I contributor during spring runoff season. Therefore, some of the recommendations in the plan did not take into account the I/I coming from high groundwater or surface water runoff events. The monitoring period that was selected for the 2006 report was also an atypically dry winter. Monitoring was conducted from December 16, 2004 through March 4, 2005. ## Infiltration and Inflow Reduction Projects

The City has completed several I/I reduction projects over the last 12 years to reduce I/I based upon the recommendations of the 2006 I/I Reduction Plan.

The first project the City completed was in 2006 and replaced approximately 1,500 LF of sewer line and eliminated one of the City’s several sewer crossings of Hangman Creek. The sewer line replaced was generally along the east side of Hangman Creek parallel to Ramsey Street. The sewer lines were reconfigured to improve flow direction and conveyance to the treatment plant. The total cost for this project was approximately $116,000.

---

In 2008, the City completed a second I/I reduction project and replaced most of the sewer infrastructure in the vicinity of Railroad Avenue and Washington Street. The project replaced one of the main sewer crossings of Hangman Creek and several manholes that were a source of I/I. Total pipe replaced for this project was approximately 1,400 LF. The cost was approximately $161,000.

The City completed a third I/I reduction project in 2012 with funding from WSDOE and CDBG. The project included replacement of nearly 3,000 LF of sewer along Hangman Creek from the WWTP to Water Street. The manholes along Hangman Creek were known to be a significant source of I/I during high creek flows and were replaced as part of the project. A portion of the sewer in Main Street was also replaced as flow monitoring and TV inspections showed signs of structural damage and significant I/I in this section. The total cost of this project was approximately $500,000.

Most recently, the City completed a large I/I reduction project in 2014 that replaced most of the remaining sewer along Hangman Creek as well as the sewer in Seamon Road and Crosby Street. A portion of the sewer utilized trenchless rehabilitation with cast in place pipe coatings because of the difficulty in accessing and replacing the sewer lines. In total, more than 5,000 LF of pipe was replaced or rehabilitated under the project. The total cost of this project was approximately $750,000.

To date, the City has replaced nearly 11,000 LF of sewer pipe and manholes to reduce I/I in the past 12 years and has seen a noticeable difference in average flows at the WWTP. The following graph illustrates the average daily flows at the plant and the changes in those flows since 2006.

## Current Monitoring Data and Analysis

To complete this analysis and report, field investigations were performed to determine appropriate locations for flow monitoring to determine the amount of I/I occurring in specific sections of the City. A field investigation was performed. The investigation included opening manholes and look for sewer lines that displayed significant sewage flows, in excess of expected flows. Four locations were identified for monitoring based on the amount of flow observed during the investigation. The four locations identified for monitoring were:

1. Manhole F1N near the intersection of Water Street and Bridge Street  
2. Manhole D1N near the intersection of Washington Street and Railroad Avenue  
3. Manhole A26 near the intersection of Park Road and Lindsey Street  
4. Manhole B7N in Seamon Road west of Howard Street  

These locations showed visible indications of higher than normal sewage flows and which were suspected to result from I/I. Several other locations were investigated including manholes on the “C” sewerline in Railroad Avenue and in the alley behind City Hall as well. With a limited flow monitoring budget, only four concurrent monitoring locations were established.


---

The monitors were installed on March 7ᵗʰ and March 8ᵗʰ, 2017. Each of the monitoring points was equipped with a level and velocity sensor and a calibrated v-notch weir to improve flow monitoring data. Site pictures and installation details for each site are included as an appendix to this report. The monitors were removed on April 18ᵗʰ, 2017 providing 25 days of concurrent monitoring data. Rain data was also collected during the flow monitoring period to determine the extent of any rainfall dependent I/I (RDII) in the system.

## Recommended Infiltration and Inflow Reduction System Improvements

The Washington State Department of Ecology is conducting a water quality study on Hangman Creek. This study is intended to assist in the development of NPDES permit limits that will protect the creek with regards to dissolved oxygen. The City also will have seasonal temperature limits. It is anticipated that major improvements will be needed at the WWTP to meet future permit requirements. It is because of these improvements that I/I reduction is so critical. Costs for treating extraneous flows at the treatment plant would likely far exceed the cost of removing the flows from the system through I/I reduction.

From analysis of the data, Century West developed several system improvements that are expected to significantly reduce the infiltration and inflow into the City sewer system.

### Sewer Line Replacements

The replacement and relocation of several sewer lines in locations that are known to have high groundwater are the highest priority for the City to reduce I/I.

During the monitoring location site investigation, it was observed that a significant portion of the D line from manhole D3 to D3.1A and above were located in a seasonal stream. The manholes for this section of sewer were made of brick and had significant leakage through the manhole walls. This section of line from manhole D3 to manhole D3.5 are recommended for replacement and relocation from the alleyway between Ramsey and Crosby to Crosby Street in order to get out of the seasonal stream. The service line to one residence on Alder Street may need to be replaced with an individual sewage pump but given the topographical relief of Crosby Street, it should be possible to connect all of the other services to the main line by gravity. It is anticipated that the relocation of this sewer line will have a significant impact on the reduction of I/I coming from the D1 service area. It is estimated that this area represented approximately 28% of the I/I recorded at the WWTP during the monitoring period.

A section of the sewer line above the A25 monitor is located in a marshy wetland area. Standing surface water was observed over the sewer line during the monitoring location site visit. It is recommended that this sewer line be relocated from the field between Park Street and Main Street into the Main Street right of way. It is not anticipated that this relocation will have any impact on sewer services along this sewer line other than relocation of one sewer service for the residence at the intersection of Lindsey Street and Main Street. Relocating this sewer line out of the marshy wetland area is anticipated to reduce I/I in this line and at the WWTP. It is estimated

---

this area represented approximately 19% of the I/I recorded at the WWTP during the monitoring period.

The sewer line that extends south from Manhole B7N in Seamon Road across the athletic fields is another priority replacement. The ground in this area is also very saturated during periods of the year and groundwater saturation is believed to cause I/I in the sewer system. The City would like to relocate this sewer line out of in the baseball field area. The sewer line would be relocated to the west in Howard Street and connection of the sewer line would be at the intersection of Seamon Road and Howard Street. The existing manhole in the infield of the baseball diamond would be removed. It is estimated that this area represented approximately 15% of the I/I recorded at the WWTP during the monitoring period.

Visual observation of flows in the sewer line in Railroad Avenue and the alley between Washington Street and Sheridan Street indicated that there is some infiltration occurring between manholes C2 and C4 that could potentially be reduced with line replacement. City maintenance personnel indicated that they would replace these lines with City forces to reduce capital outlay costs.

The monitor at Manhole F1N indicated a high percentage of I/I in the system above this manhole but given the large contributing area, an improvement project to reduce I/I in this area is not recommended at this time. It is estimated that the amount of I/I coming from this area was approximately 6% of the total I/I at the treatment plant during the monitoring period. Further study of the contribution in this area in the future to narrow the sewer replacement and target I/I areas before capital dollars are invested is recommended.

Planning level cost estimates for the sewers are provided in the appendix of this report.

# Conclusion
The City sewer system has significant Infiltration and Inflow issues that need to be addressed. Four locations were monitored under this study and approximately 69% of the seasonal I/I recorded at the WWTP appears to come from the contributing areas above these locations. Century West recommends replacement of sewers above 3 of the monitoring locations (A25, D1 and B7N) to reduce I/I and replacement of another section of sewer (C lines) to further reduce I/I. After replacement of these sewer lines, monitoring and analysis of WWTP flows as well as other potential sources of I/I should continue to determine the extent of I/I reduction resulting from the improvements. Further monitoring can also be used to determine if further I/I reduction is needed.

## 2. Flow Monitoring Stations

Prior to installing flow monitoring stations, SFE performed detailed site assessments of each potential site to determine the appropriate monitoring method. Factors such as pipe size, channel condition, site location, site access, and flow hydraulics were all considered and documented while performing site assessments.

The meters were calibrated and set to log data at 5 minute intervals as per spec and standard SFE procedure. To ensure proper operation of the stations, a regular maintenance schedule is adhered to for the duration of the project with bi-weekly site visits. During install, a preset benchmark is established for flow levels and during each visit this “field level” is compared to the meter readings simultaneously and recorded on the site maintenance sheet. All sites have shown to be well within limitations at this point in the project.

## 4. Conclusion

All sites showed definite reaction to rainfall with 1 and 2 showing surcharge during the heavy storm events in first 2 weeks of the project. Data has been edited during these surcharges to use AV flow formula due to the Weir itself being maxed out and the water not free flowing, as is required with Weir flow. We use the Velocity sensor with our loggers as a redundancy for just this purpose.  
Site 4 had some issues with regard to the flowmeter itself shutting down and losing data in March. The meter was replaced after this failure and then the new meter again shutdown on April 10 until removal. We can only attribute this issue to site conditions with high moisture being the suspected culprit. Sites were slated to be removed on April 7 so data was collected at this site up to the initial end date.  
No data continuity issues are noticed at the other 3 sites.  
All sites were removed on April 17 and all data to this point has been made available in Summary form.

# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This Infiltration and Inflow Study has been prepared for the City of Tekoa to evaluate the incidence of infiltration and inflow into the City’s sewage collection system and make recommendations regarding the future of those facilities. The wastewater treatment plant has been experiencing flows greater than capacity. The first portion of the collection system was constructed in the early 1900’s and the last portion was constructed in the 1940’s. The treatment plant was constructed in 1950.

The collection system consists of six subsystems, the “A” Line through the “F” Line. The majority of the system is made up of six and eight-inch diameter pipe. With the exception of PVC and PCC pipe installed in recent construction projects and during periodic maintenance the pipe material consists of the original vitrified clay. The original manholes were of brick and mortar. More recent manholes are of pre-cast and cast-in-place concrete.

There are five creek crossings in the collection system, three of Hangman Creek and two of Little Hangman Creek. A construction project in 2005 eliminated the crossing of Hangman Creek at the footbridge opposite Main Street. Approximately 5,500 feet of the “A” Line subsystem parallels Hangman Creek and approximately 2,200 feet of the “B” Line subsystem parallels Little Hangman Creek. Portions of both subsystems lie within the floodplains of the creeks.

Flowmeters were placed at several key manholes where the instantaneous flow for each subsystem in the collection system could be measured. Flow monitoring was done from mid-December 2004 to early May 2005. The flowmeters were placed at the following key manholes:

* A1, A5 (on both lines into the manhole), A11, A12, A16, A21, B2, B3, B7, C2, D2, E1 (on both lines into the manhole), F1 and F2.

A survey to locate the manholes was performed to produce an accurate map of the collection system and to provide information for input into the flow model. The majority of the “A” and “B” lines and portions of the “C”, “D” and “E” lines were video-inspected, representing approximately 25% of the collection system. Examination of the videotapes revealed numerous locations of root penetration at joints, deformations in pipes including broken sections, groundwater infiltration and at one creek crossing where creek water was entering the pipe.

Collection zones were identified based on the flow monitoring locations. The flow model that was developed utilized the monitoring data to estimate the amount of I/I in the collection zones. The flow model for the collection system was created using HYDRA computer software. The resulting calculations of the flow data indicate the collection system experiences an estimated additional 0.260 MGD into the treatment plant from I/I. This is the amount of I/I estimated at the last manhole before the lift station to the treatment plant, MH A1, and is the approximate difference between the Manning’s formula calculation of the flow monitoring data and the model flow.

---

A list of recommended improvements for the collection system has been developed based on the amount of estimated I/I in a collection zone. The improvements include the following portions of the collection system:

* Collection Zone 2: the lines from MH C1 to MH C5.1, from MH C1 to north of MH D2, from MH C5 to MH C11, from MH C9 to MH 9.2 and from MH E3 to MH E4. MH E3 to MH E4 is in Collection Zone 8 but is included here because it has been identified in this study as a serious concern that should be addressed immediately.
* Collection Zone 7: the lines from MH A6 to MH A11 and the lateral to MH A10.2.
* Collection Zone 5: the lines from MH B3 to MH 7.2, from MH B4 to MH B4.4 and from MH B5 to south of MH B5.3.
* Collection Zone 12: the line from MH AA6 to MH A16.
* Collection Zone 9: the lines from MH A16 to MH A21 and from MH A20 to MH A20.3.

Collection zones with higher estimated rates of I/I are included in the listed improvements. The goal of the improvements presented is to reduce the I/I by an amount that would lower the flow to the treatment plant to below its capacity.

# I. INTRODUCTION

The City of Tekoa owns, operates and maintains a sanitary sewer system and sewage treatment facility. The general age of the sanitary sewer system is approximately 50 years. City maintenance crews have observed deterioration in the pipe and manholes of the system. Deterioration noted includes cracked and displaced pipes and missing brick and mortar in the manholes.

## A. Authorization

In December 2004, the City Council of Tekoa authorized Century West Engineering Corporation, as the City's Engineering Consultant, to proceed with the preparation of an Infiltration and Inflow (I/I) Study. In July 2005, the City further authorized preparation of a Comprehensive Sewer and Facilities Plan. This I/I study will provide analysis of the condition of the wastewater collection system which will be used in the comprehensive sewer plan.

This planning document is funded through a grant from Community Development Block Grant Program, a loan from the Public Works Trust Fund and City Funds.

The scope of this planning document has been developed to allow the plan to meet the requirements for a General Sewer Plan under WAC 173-240-050.

## B. Background and Purpose

This investigation was required by the Department of Ecology (DOE) in an agreement to bring the collection system and wastewater treatment facility into compliance with the City’s NPDES permit. Currently the City’s wastewater treatment facility experiences flows that exceed the flows that would be expected for a city of its size. The treatment plant experiences significant flows during high precipitation events. The main line that carries the bulk of the flow from the various subsystems of the collection system, the “A” Line, parallels and is adjacent to Hangman Creek. In addition, the trunk line of the “B” Line is parallel and adjacent to Little Hangman Creek. The inverts of both lines are considerably below water level during runoff events from precipitation or melting snow. Portions of both lines are within the 100-year floodplain. Inundation from flooding is possible on the “A” Line from Manhole A1 to Manhole A17, Manholes B3 and B7 on the “B” Line and Manhole C1 on the “C” Line. Maps 1 and 2 in Appendix D illustrate the existing sanitary sewer system in the City of Tekoa.

This planning document is necessary to provide the City with the background information it needs to prepare the Comprehensive Sewer Plan and Wastewater Facilities Plan and make sound decisions about the future of the collection system and treatment facility.

The purpose of this Infiltration and Inflow investigation is to identify the primary sources and magnitude of extraneous surface and groundwater flows entering the sanitary sewer collection system. This document will be used to quantify the extent of infiltration and

inflow remediation that is feasible. To that end, improvements to the collection system are recommended based on the information and data developed and the associated costs are included in this study. This will provide guidance to the City on which areas of the collection system they should focus their I/I reduction efforts.

# Field Investigations

The City of Tekoa employed several field investigation methods to determine causes of infiltration and inflow into their sewer collection system. The methods that the City used included a flow monitoring program with flowmeters placed at key manholes where the instantaneous flow for each subsystem in the collection system could be measured, smoke testing of sewer lines, a TV inspection of several of the major sewer trunks and visual inspections of the sewer system components that were accessible.

The sewer lines were not cleaned prior to flow monitoring. The lines that were video-taped were however, flushed prior to video inspection. Additional cleaning was necessary in some of those lines due to the quantity of rock, silt and roots.

## Flow Monitoring

The City of Tekoa rented three flow monitoring units from American Sigma Inc. to monitor certain major trunk sewer lines in their collection system. The rented units were Sigma 910 flow meters, each with an Area-Velocity flow monitoring probe. This flow monitoring probe uses a pressure sensing disk that records minute changes in pressure that occur when the depth of the sewer flow changes over the sensor. Using a proprietary algorithm, the flow monitor equates the pressure recorded to a depth of flow and records that depth every 15 minutes. Concurrently, the velocity sensor portion of the probe uses a Doppler radar system that calculates the average velocity of the sewer flow by sending Doppler radar pulses into the flow and recording the time it takes from the pulse origination for the pulse to rebound off of a particle and return to the sensor. The software provided by American Sigma calculates the area of flow in the pipe based on the pipe diameter and the depth of flow in the pipe. The software uses the calculated flow area and the recorded velocity to compute the flow rate in the sewer pipe.

The Sigma 910 monitors used for this analysis appeared to have problems with the velocity component of the Area-Velocity sensor. The velocity recorded by the monitor was not consistent and often dropped to zero. To alleviate this problem, a manual Manning's calculation was done for each of the flow monitoring points and compared to the flow generated by the monitor calculations. For several of the monitors, the Manning's calculation was very different from the monitor Area-Velocity calculation. The Manning's calculation appears to follow a reasonable diurnal variation trend and produces average flowrates that are fairly consistent with the City of Tekoa Treatment plant flow monitoring records and was used in the development of the flow model.

Manning's calculation uses several variables in calculating the amount of flow in a sewer pipe. These variables include the sewer flow depth, the Manning's roughness coefficient, and the slope of the section of pipe. The flow monitoring equipment recorded the depth of flow in the sewer pipe for the calculation. The Manning's roughness is an empirical number that varies between 0.012 and 0.015 for concrete and vitrified clay sewer pipe. The Manning's roughness used in this analysis was 0.013. The slope used in each of the Manning's calculation was calculated using the information recorded by the recent survey of the manholes in the City of Tekoa. The survey of the manholes included a measurement of the invert elevation of each manhole in the City and the

---

distance between the manholes to determine the length of pipe between manholes. The slope  
was calculated by finding the invert elevation difference between two manholes and dividing by  
the length of pipe between them.  

The precipitation data used in this analysis was recorded in the City of Tekoa on a daily basis.  
The data is somewhat limited based on the recording interval of one record every 24 hours, hence  
the depiction on the following graphs as flat lines. The sewer flow monitoring also occurred  
during a period of time in which there was very little precipitation. The lack of precipitation led  
to the declaration of a drought. The limited rainfall data that was obtained was used to determine  
whether any rainfall dependant infiltration or inflow was occurring in the collection system above  
the monitored location

### Manhole A12 to Manhole A13

Figure 1 in Appendix B shows the monitoring data recorded at MH A12 for the pipe connecting MH A12 to MH A13. This monitor was installed on January 7ᵗʰ, 2005 and was removed on January 21ˢᵗ, 2005. The Manning's calculation for this monitor used a computed slope of 0.015 ft/ft and a pipe diameter of 8".

This pipe experienced several periods of surcharge where the level monitored in the pipe was greater than the diameter of the pipe. This is measurable with the pressure sensor probe used in this monitoring effort. The sensor calculates the equivalent depth of water over the sensor, even if the pipe is in a full or surcharged state. Around January 18ᵗʰ, 2005, this monitor started recording depth of flow readings that indicated a surcharged pipe. ### Manhole A11 to Manhole A12

Figure 3 in Appendix B is a graph of the flow monitoring data recorded at MH A11. Flow at this location was monitored from February 4ᵗʰ to February 18ᵗʰ, 2005. For this monitoring point, a computed slope of 0.0079 ft/ft and a diameter of 8" was used. The Manning's calculation

---

follows a general diurnal pattern and thus was used in the analysis of flow at this location. The line between these manholes crosses Hangman Creek and is suspected of being a source of I/I.

One point of concern in the monitoring data for this location is the upward trend of the flow rate that appears to somewhat level off near the end. This upward trend could be caused by one of two things. The first possibility is that the level recorded, and therefore the flow rate, does actually increase and a significantly higher flow rate is recorded at the end of the monitoring period than was recorded at the beginning. The second possibility is that the pressure depth sensor went through an adjustment period. This upward trend could have been the sensor acclimating to a new monitoring point or it could have been caused by an obstruction to the sensor port of the pressure sensor.

More likely, it is a result of the pressure sensor adjusting to a new monitoring point and not an actual increase in sewer flows. If it were an actual increase in sewer flows, the diurnal variation in the recorded depth would be as apparent and pronounced at the start of the monitoring data as it is near the end of the monitoring period. This monitoring data shows erratic readings for the beginning of the monitoring period that eventually converge into a consistent diurnal pattern at the end of the monitoring period, making the second possibility, that of an adjustment period, the more likely. It is suggested for future flow monitoring that the calibration and monitoring be done after the sensor has been installed for a period of time.

After the monitoring took place, this line was plugged and abandoned as part of a sewer improvement project and is no longer in service. The connection between MH A13 and MH A12 was severed with the installation of a new manhole identified as MH AA6 and the installation of a new sewer line that was connected to existing MH B3. This newly installed line is identified on Maps 1 and 2 in Appendix D as the line from MH AA1 through MH AA6.

This change in the collection system renders the flow data at this monitoring point out of date and any I/I estimated to be occurring in this section of the system could be considered eliminated. This will be discussed further in Chapter 2.

**Manhole A5 to Manhole B1**

Figure 5 in Appendix B is a graph of the flow data recorded in MH A5 for the pipe between MH A5 and MH B1. This monitor was in place between December 16th, 2004 and December 28th, 2004. The monitor Area-Velocity calculation produced similar results as the Manning's calculation for this monitor. The Manning's calculation is a little more consistent and not as sporadic as the Area-Velocity calculation and follows a good diurnal variation.

There was not enough rainfall during this monitoring period to determine if precipitation contributes significantly to sewage flows at this monitoring point. The rainfall recorded over the monitoring period amounted to 0.03".

# Smoke Testing

## Smoke Testing 2003

In July 2003, the City of Tekoa, with Kelly Willquit of Evergreen Rural Water performed a series of smoke tests on some of the sewer system manholes with a potential for I/I problems. These smoke tests revealed some possible I/I sources in the following locations:

- In MH A1, an opening was observed leading to a tunnel on the south side of the manhole. This tunnel appeared to be 2" to 4" in diameter and approximately 12" above the level Hangman Creek at the time of testing. It was thought at the time of the smoke testing that this tunnel could be a significant source of I/I if the creek were to rise above the invert elevation. The tunnel however, is no longer open to the creek. MH A1 is the last manhole in the collection system prior to the lift station at the treatment plant. This tunnel was originally a bypass for sewage overflow when the flow into the plant became too great for the lift station pumps to handle.

- A number of holes were observed in the side of MH A6. These could allow infiltration when the ground is saturated. It is recommended that the manhole be repaired.

- A significant number of holes were also observed in MH A7 as well as severe erosion around it. This could be a result of flow from Hangman Creek passing around the manhole. The eroded area was filled with rock.

- Severe problems inside and out of MH A11 were observed. Mortar was observed falling out from between the bricks inside the manhole. This could eventually cause a failure of the structure. The mortar outside of the manhole was also cracked and crumbling off. This manhole needed extensive repair in 2003. Repair of this manhole was not done. This manhole was abandoned as part of the sewer improvement project mentioned previously.

- Manhole A12 was determined to be a significant source of I/I when it was smoke tested. It was estimated that nearly 90% of the ground around the manhole had been eroded by flow in Hangman Creek. During periods of high water in Hangman Creek the manhole became an island in the stream flow around it. During smoke testing, the ground 5'-10' away released smoke everywhere the ground had eroded. This manhole was removed from service in the previously mentioned sewer project. That project placed a new manhole designated MH AA6 between MH A12 and MH A13 and abandoned and plugged the sewer crossing between MH A12 and MH A11.

- Cracks and holes were observed between the bricks of MH A13 at the time of testing. Smoke was observed filtering through the soil around the manhole when it was smoke tested. This manhole was estimated to be far enough away from the creek that I/I would only occur on high water runoff days of the winter and spring.

- Manholes A14, A15, and A16 were not observed to be causes of I/I during smoke testing. The rim elevation of these manholes was observed to be near or at high water elevation of the creek.

# 2. System Analysis

## Sewer Flow Rates and Model Analysis

Part of the analysis for this infiltration and inflow report was to create a model of the City of Tekoa's sewer system. The sewer system model was used to analyze sewage flow in the collection system in order to estimate locations where infiltration was occurring in the system.

HYDRA software was utilized in creating this sewer model. City personnel performed field investigations to record the depth of each manhole from the rim to the bottom of the pipe. The manholes were then surveyed using GPS (Global Positioning System) equipment to determine the rim elevation of every manhole and the length of each run of sewer pipe in the sewer system. The depth from the rim measured by City personnel was then subtracted from the surveyed rim elevation to determine the invert elevation of the pipes in the sewer system. The invert elevations and the sewer pipe lengths were input into the HYDRA model. Pipe slope was calculated from this information. Pipe diameters were obtained from City documentation and from the TV inspection recordings and input in the HYDRA model as well.

The City provided sewer billing addresses and a list of commercial businesses on the City sewer system. These addresses were mapped out on the City of Tekoa's sewer system map to determine the number of ERU's (Equivalent Residential Units) that were contributing sewage flow to each run of sewer pipe. One ERU was estimated to be the equivalent sewer generation of 2.5 persons in a residential home. Commercial businesses were assigned a number of ERU's based on the business type and the estimated sewer generation for the business. Restaurants, for example, were assigned 3 ERU's. This approach accounts for the increased sewer flows from a commercial business that would generate more sewer flow than a single family home. A summary of the commercial businesses in the City of Tekoa and the number of ERU's that were estimated to be generated from them is listed in Table 2.1.

## Daily Infiltration/Inflow Rates

The following discussion of the monitoring data collected by the flowmeters is concerned with estimating the daily infiltration and inflow into the City’s sanitary sewer system and begins at the upstream monitoring location of the A Line subsystem, moving downstream through all the subsystems to Manhole A1, which is the last manhole prior to the treatment plant lift station. The flowmeters were placed at key manholes where the instantaneous flow for each subsystem could be measured. Infiltration influenced by creek flows was discussed in the previous chapter and is discussed briefly again at the end of this chapter.

---

The amount of ground or surface water is dependent upon the time of year and the amount of precipitation during the monitoring period. The Tekoa sewer system was monitored from mid-December to early March, a snapshot in time (see Table 1.1). Monitoring over a different time frame would likely produce different results. Consequently, the calculation of I/I can only be an approximation.

Using the model, an amount of I/I is estimated for each section of pipe between monitoring locations. Because the amount at each downstream monitoring location is accumulative of all upstream flow, the amount, or amounts, of I/I estimated at each upstream location is subtracted from the accumulative total at the downstream location to establish a value at the downstream location.

The flow data for this study was collected using three flowmeters. Each location was monitored for a two-week period and there were six two-week periods. The time periods can be seen in Table 1.1 of the previous section. Ideally, to obtain the best results from the data collected, every key manhole would be monitored over the same time period. Then, I/I would be recorded under the same conditions (i.e., groundwater level, streamflow, precipitation events) for the entire system. However, the results of the model will still be an approximation due to the length of the monitoring period and time of the year the monitoring took place.

Monitoring three locations at a time does not allow the flow to be recorded under the same set of conditions. Given that flow for the Tekoa sewer system was monitored over different time periods, the method used to determine the estimated I/I at each monitoring location, that of subtracting the I/I estimated at the upstream location from the downstream location, introduces some additional imprecision. Although determining the flow at the downstream locations in this way adds to the approximate nature of the calculation to some degree, it is seen as an inconsequential increase and thus is considered acceptable as a method of estimating the amount of I/I in the sewer system.

The graphs referenced in the following discussion of daily I/I rates compare the Manning's flow calculations presented in Chapter 1 with the model flow calculations based on 75 gpd per capita. Plots of the two calculations are overlaid upon each other. The gap between the two plotted lines represents the amount of I/I.

## Manhole A11 to Manhole A12

As can be seen with Figure 6 in Appendix C, the monitoring data at this point had a large variation from the initial readings through to the last, exhibiting an upward trend in flow. It was difficult to determine the normal flow rate recorded at this monitor and subsequently difficult to determine the I/I occurring upstream of this location.

---

The steadily increasing flow rate was discussed in Chapter 1. There are three possibilities as to the cause of the upward drifting data at the manhole. The first two were discussed in Chapter 1. They are: 1) that the recording is accurate and the flow rate did increase over the monitoring period or 2) that the pressure depth sensor went through an adjustment period. A third possibility considered in this analysis is that that there was some sort of partial sewer blockage just downstream of the level sensor of the monitor that caused flow to back up into the pipe.

The first possibility can be verified by comparing the data to downstream monitors to determine if an increase in base wastewater flow is recorded downstream of this monitoring point. The data recorded at this location should be approximately equal to the sum of the flow monitoring data recorded upstream of this location. Figure 7 in Appendix C shows the summation of the upstream monitors versus the upward trending flow data recorded at MH A11. The summation of the upstream monitors produces a curve similar to the initial monitored readings by the flow monitor. Figure 8 in Appendix C shows the average daily flow at the Tekoa treatment plant and the monitoring data at a point downstream of MH A11 confirming our conclusion in Chapter 1 that the increase in base flow is not a real phenomenon.

The second possibility, that the flow monitoring sensor experienced a "drifting" period was considered the most likely cause in Chapter 1. The final possibility, that there was a partial sewer line blockage downstream of the level sensor is less likely because, as stated above, the level readings would increase for a partial blockage (which they did not do) but the flow rate would not increase as the Manning’s calculation indicates. Therefore, as stated in Chapter 1, the most likely cause of the incorrect flow increase shown on the figures for this monitor is a malfunctioning level sensor.

The initial readings provided a sufficient amount of data that could be used to estimate the I/I upstream of MH A11 as approximately 0.09 mgd. The I/I estimated includes 0.01 mgd estimated for the sewer lines above MH E1.1, 0.01 mgd estimated for the sewer lines above MH E2, and 0.03 mgd for the sewer lines above MH A12 along the A line. This leaves approximately 0.04 mgd of I/I between MH A11 and MH A12. The sewer line that connects MH A11 and MH A12 crosses underneath Hangman Creek and is suspected of being a significant source of I/I into that line.

As mentioned in Chapter 1, subsequent to this monitoring period, the City of Tekoa executed a contract to abandon the sewer line between MH A11 and MH A12. The flows were diverted from a point in between MH A13 and MH A12 to a series of new manholes and eventually MH B3. According to the calculations presented herein, this project should have eliminated approximately 0.04 mgd of I/I. More flow monitoring would be necessary to determine the effect that these improvements had on the infiltration to the sewer system. Given the lack of precipitation during the monitoring period for this study it was decided not to adjust the overall amount of estimated I/I resulting from the system improvements.

# APPENDIX A

## River Influence Analysis

From the results of this study it is estimated that the City of Tekoa sewer system experiences on the average 0.26 mgd of infiltration and inflow. The data collected during the study also suggests that a significant amount of the measured I/I comes from the creeks. The A and B line sections of the sewer collection system are adjacent to Hangman Creek and Little Hangman Creek. Some portions of these lines are within identified floodplains. Based on the condition of these sewer lines and measured flows, infiltration into the system is apparent when the water levels in the creeks are higher than these sewer lines.

It was noted in Chapter 1 that the monitoring data shows flows at MH A21 increased around the time of a period of precipitation. The increase in flow is suspected to be caused by high creek flows. The goal of the following analysis is to establish a connection between the increase in monitored flows and high creek flows caused by precipitation events.

Since no streamflow data was readily available for Hangman Creek at Tekoa, streamflow data for the Palouse River was used in this analysis. The streamflow monitoring point on the Palouse River nearest Tekoa was determined from a listing on the USGS water resources webpage at waterdata.usgs.gov. This monitoring point is located approximately 25 miles south and east of Tekoa. The headwaters of Hangman Creek are approximately 13 miles from the Palouse River monitoring station. Given this proximity it is reasonable to assume that rainfall and snowmelt over the region would contribute to flows in both Hangman Creek and the Palouse River during the same period of time.

To validate the assumption that the streamflow characteristics in Hangman Creek are similar to the streamflow in the Palouse River, monitoring data for Hangman Creek was obtained from a monitoring point near Spokane, WA. The streamflow monitoring location for Hangman Creek in Spokane is approximately 40 miles northwest of the City of Tekoa. The streamflow data from the Palouse River was compared to streamflow data for Hangman Creek. A graph showing the comparison of data for these two monitoring locations can be found on Figure A-1.

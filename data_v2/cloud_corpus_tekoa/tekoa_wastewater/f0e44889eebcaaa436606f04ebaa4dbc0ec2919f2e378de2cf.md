# INTRODUCTION

In 1990, the City of Tekoa undertook major renovations to the municipal wastewater treatment system. Several improvements were made at the wastewater treatment plant and approximately 480 LF of sewer line was replaced in town to eliminate high infiltration.

This **appendix does not replace the existing sewage treatment plant O&M Manual. It should be used in conjunction with the existing manual.** The **addendum** describes the 1990 modifications and their effect on the operation and maintenance to the treatment plant.

The Tekoa wastewater treatment system is classified as a complete mix activated sludge process. A revised system diagram, flow schematic, and site piping location map are included in this addendum as Figures 1, 2, and 3. The design parameters for all processes and operations making up the system are given in Table III of this addendum.

---

# INTRODUCTION (cont.)

The major elements of the 1990 treatment plant modifications include:

## 1. Lift Station Renovation
* Three new "Hydromatic" 10 hp centrifugal pumps were installed with a guide rail system.
* The wet well was sandblasted and cleaned.
* "I" beam supports were added under the lift station floor.
* Explosion-proof lighting was installed.
* The pump station electrical control system was modified or replaced as necessary to accommodate the replacement pumps.

## 2. Hydroscreens
* A prefabricated building was constructed with lighting and heating.
* Two 900 gpm hydroscreens were installed to remove solids and coarse debris.
* Two dumpsters were provided, each having a capacity of 1 cubic yard.

## 3. Ultrasonic Flowmeter
* A "Milltronics Multiranger" ultrasonic flowmeter with digital readout, chart recorder, and transducer was installed to measure treatment system effluent flow rate. This unit measures flow and signals the automatic flow proportional controllers for the chlorination and dechlorination equipment.

## 4. Dechlorination System
* A concrete block dechlorination/polymer building was constructed. The building includes heating, lighting, exhaust fan and emergency SO₂ system failure alarm light.
* A Wallace & Tiernan Model V-500 Sulfonator dechlorination control unit was installed with an injector unit, diffuser, and miscellaneous equipment and fittings. The unit is equipped with a flow proportional controller.

# OPERATION AND CONTROL

## 2.1 - PUMP STATION (Modified 1990)

A 12" gravity main flows to the pump station. Raw sewage is pumped from the wet well to the hydroscreens. The pump station is equipped with 3 submersible pumps, each capable of pumping 3" spheres and long stringy solids. Each of the pumps can be pulled for maintenance using the manual hoist centered over the opening in the floor above the wet well. A lifting chain is permanently attached to each pump.

The pumps are oil filled with a seal leak detection probe. Seal leak warning lights are located at the pump control panel. This will alert maintenance personnel of lower seal failure without having to take the unit out of service for inspection or requiring access for checking seal chamber oil level consistency.

During normal operation, the lead pump should alternate at least every 24 hours.

Appendix "A" of this addendum provides a description of hazards associated with lift station wet well maintenance.

Appendix "B" of this addendum gives detailed descriptive and operational information from the manufacturer.

## 2.2 HYDROSCREENS - (Installed 1990)

Preliminary screening by hydroscreens removes organic and inorganic solids and coarse debris. This reduces loading and maintenance on digesters, clarifiers, piping, pumps and improves total system efficiency.

There are two hydroscreens designed for flows of 900 gpm each. Solids removed by the screens drop into dumpsters which should be emptied at least once each week. During normal operation, each dumpster should fill to one-quarter capacity in a one week period. Both screens should be operated to evenly distribute solids between the two dumpsters.

The hydroscreens will require cleaning with a broom 2-3 times daily to remove grit buildup. To remove fiber and grease buildup, the screens should be steam cleaned or pressure washed on a monthly basis.

Never use a wire brush when cleaning the screens.

For further detailed descriptive and operational information from the manufacturer, refer to Appendix "C".

# 2.3 AERATION BASIN AND CLARIFIER - (Modified 1990)

The only noteworthy aspect of the additional transfer pipe installed between the aeration basin and the clarifier is that the valve in that pipe is designated as No. 10a and that it should be closed only when valve No. 10 is closed. The purpose of this transfer pipe is to prevent aeration basin overflow during periods of high flow.

# 2.4 CHLORINE CONTACT TANK - (Modified 1990)

During the 1990 plant improvements, the concrete bridge was repaired, a sulfur dioxide dechlorination diffuser was installed, and an ultrasonic flow meter was installed.

# 2.5 AEROBIC DIGESTER - (Modified 1990)

When the contents of the aerobic digester reaches the level of the overflow pipes, a portion of the liquid must be decanted. The floating aerator is turned off and the solids allowed to settle. When the clarified liquid extends one to two feet deep, Valve No. 24 is opened and the supernatant decanted by gravity to the wet well. When solids concentration in the aerobic digester approaches 2.0 percent, decanting will no longer be possible as the sludge will not settle to a higher solids concentration. This can be determined by sampling and testing. Sludge should then be wasted to one of the sludge drying beds. This is accomplished by first closing valve No. 18. Valve No. 16 should then be opened. Valve Nos. 19, 20 or 20a should be opened until the desired volume of sludge has been discharged onto the drying bed. Note that polymer feed must occur during sludge wasting. Therefore, it will probably be necessary to throttle the flow with the valves at the drying beds.

After sludge has been wasted from the digester, first close Valve No. 16 and then open Valve No. 18. This will allow sludge in the pipelines to flow back to the lift station and prevent the lines from freezing in the winter.

The digester mounted hoist/crain will facilitate removal and reinstallation of the mixer aeration when it becomes necessary to maintenance that unit. Appendix "H" of this addendum gives detailed descriptive and operational information from the manufacturer.

# 2.6 POLYMER FEED SYSTEM - (Installed 1990)

In general, the polymer feed equipment consists of a 500 gallon fiberglass mixing tank, a polymer mixer and a polymer feed pump.

---

## 2.6 POLYMER FEED SYSTEM - (Continued)

Spilled, wet polymer can produce very slippery conditions which can cause injury to operating personnel due to falling. Close attention to housekeeping practices to keep areas clean and dry will minimize these hazards.

Polymer is used to change the electrical charge of solid particles so that solids attract each other. When this occurs, it is much easier for water to drain from the mixed sludge and flow through the drying bed media filter. A 0.5 percent to 1 percent polymer mixture is normally used when mixing the polymer with the sludge.

The amount of polymer mixed will depend on the amount of sludge to waste from the aerobic digester. Generally, if 10,000 gallons of sludge is to be wasted, a 100 gallon mixture of 0.5% to 1% polymer should be prepared. This is accomplished by filling the mixing tank to the 100 gallon calibration and adding one half to one gallon of liquid polymer. The exact amount of polymer used will depend upon experience with the system. It is recommended that a 0.5% polymer mixture be used as a minimum base amount.

Note that one foot of depth in the sand drying bed is 6,500 gallons while one foot of depth in one of the media drying beds is 3,100 gallons. After the polymer has been added to the correct amount of water, the polymer mixer should be turned on and run until the polymer is evenly dispersed. The polymer mixture is ready to be added to the wasted sludge.

The polymer feed pump is a dual head variable speed pump with a maximum pumping capacity of 200 gallons per hour (gph). At a minimum, the polymer feed pump setting should be 1 percent of the sludge flow to the drying beds. For example, if sludge is flowing to the drying beds at approximately 60 gallons per minute, this is equal to 3,600 gph. The polymer feed pump should be set at 36 gallons per hour for one head only.

To ensure adequate mixing of the sludge and polymer, it is recommended that the polymer feed pump setting equal 2 percent of the sludge flow to the drying beds utilizing a 0.5 percent polymer mixture. For example, when wasting 5,000 gallons of sludge over a one hour period, the following steps should be taken:

* a. Add 100 gallons of water with one-half gallon of polymer in the mixing tank and blend well.
* b. Be sure valves to the drying beds (19, 20 or 20a) are closed.
* c. Close Valve No. 18.
* d. Open Valve No. 16 at digester.

---

# 2.7 SLUDGE DRYING BEDS - (Installed 1990)

Sludge is wasted to the sludge drying beds when solids concentration in the aerobic digester approaches 2.0%. Sludge is wasted by opening Valve No. 16 and wasting to one of the three beds by opening Valve Nos. 19, 20, or 20a. When wasting into the media filter drying beds, a 4 to 6" layer of sludge is recommended and will provide wasting volume of 1023 gallons and 1550 gallons, respectively. This will allow for the most efficient drying time. Sludge should be able to be transported in 3-4 days drying time.

When enough sludge is wasted, close Valve No. 16 and open Valve No. 18 to allow the pipe to completely drain. The liquid portion of the wasted sludge will seep to the drain system under the bed and will be returned to the wet well by gravity. The remaining sludge "cake" can be removed from the media bed using a small rubber tire loader (Bobcat) or by hand shovel. Sludge should only be removed from the sand bed by hand shovel or fork. Traffic in the sand by rubber-tired vehicles will damage the bed. Sludge production is not expected to exceed 0.1 lbs per capita per day.

For further information regarding the O & M of the media filter, refer to Appendix "F".

## 2.8 FLOW MONITORING, CHLORINATION & DECHLORINATION - (Modified 1990)

A Milltronics Multiranger ultrasonic flow meter was installed in the polymer building which regulates the automatic flow proportional controllers for the chlorination and dechlorination systems. The transducer for the flow meter is located in the chlorine contact basin. As flows fluctuate, the ultrasonic flow meter will adjust the chlorination and dechlorination injection proportionately. The unit is calibrated to monitor flows from 0-1 million gpd. The flow meter has an 8 digit totalizer used to indicate total flow. A 32 day strip chart recorder is included to monitor flow fluctuations.

The chlorination unit for the Tekoa Treatment Facility is a Wallace and Tiernan V-800 chlorinator which has been converted to a remote vacuum configuration. An automatic flow proportional control has been installed which is regulated by signals from the ultrasonic flow metering system. An automatic tank switchover for the chlorine gas cylinders was also added to the system. A new intake fan was installed in the chlorine room. The 300 cfm intake fan is electrically controlled by the chlorine room light switch. The switch is located outside of the door.

At the present time, the equipment required to monitor for SO₂ residual is not available to the treatment plant operator; therefore the SO₂ feed rate is adjusted by monitoring the residual chlorine concentration in the effluent (i.e., if chlorine residuals begin showing up in the effluent, the SO₂ feed rate is increased until chlorine residuals are no longer detected; the SO₂ feed rate is kept higher than the break even point to ensure dechlorination). It is realized that the detection limit for chlorine is above the threshold for toxic effects, and that it is more effective for the operator to monitor for SO₂ residual to ensure dechlorination; therefore, it has been recommended to the City that SO₂ monitoring equipment be purchased as soon as the funds are available.

The chlorination rate should be adjusted so that the concentration in the effluent prior to dechlorination is less than 2 ppm. Bacti sampling for effluent monitoring should be taken ahead of the dechlorination point. If bacti testing indicates that inadequate disinfection is occurring, the chlorination rate should be increased in small increments until adequate disinfection is taking place.

The dechlorination system sulfonator with flow proportional controller consists of a Wallace and Tiernan V-500 for the delivery of sulfur dioxide. The dechlorination injection rate should be approximately 50 percent of the chlorine injection rate for the proper neutralization of residual chlorine. A two cylinder Wallace and Tiernan scale and automatic tank switch are used for the sulfur dioxide gas cylinders.

## 2.9 YARD HYDRANTS (Modified 1990)

The treatment plant site is provided with a number of yard hydrants for wash down purposes. The entire treatment plant water system is protected by a reduced pressure principal backflow prevention device located in the chlorine room. Each yard hydrant and the lab sink is protected with a vacuum breaker to prevent back siphoning. The yard hydrants are "frost-free" and drain automatically when shut off; however, the vacuum breakers must be purged manually to allow the hydrants to drain.

# MAINTENANCE

## 3.1 MECHANICAL EQUIPMENT

The operator’s primary responsibility is to provide the best possible treatment. To accomplish this, it will be necessary for the operator to minimize unscheduled equipment breakdown and failure through the implementation of an organized and scheduled preventive maintenance program. A preventive maintenance tasks schedule for the 1990 additions and modifications is included with this addendum as Table I. Note that the schedule is cross-referenced with the appendices of this addendum to allow the operator to easily locate the applicable manufacturer’s information regarding the indicated tasks. Table II gives a listing of the suppliers for the various items of hardware and equipment that were included in the 1990 modifications to the facility.

## 3.2 COLLECTOR/INTERCEPTOR SYSTEM

Sewer inspections should be conducted at least once a year. An inspection should consist of opening up several manholes throughout the system and observing the flow in the sewer and the condition (water tightness) of the manhole. In making such an inspection, it is not necessary to enter the manhole unless a problem is detected and it becomes necessary to make a closer inspection. If manholes are entered, the safety procedures suggested in Appendix “A” should be followed. Standing wastewater in a manhole indicates that the downstream sewer is clogged. Also sluggish moving wastewater or a scum on the water surface is an indication of clogging. Accumulations of sludge on bottom of the manhole or high water marks on the walls is an indication of cyclic flooding caused by a partial obstruction in the sewer lines below.

Inspections should also be performed if unusually high flows are occurring at the treatment plant. The part of the system that is causing the high flows can be easily found by looking into key manholes when the high flows are occurring.

> **Flow Description:**

- Wastewater flows from Hangman Creek into the Chlorine Contact Basin.
- From the Chlorine Contact Basin, flow proceeds to the Clarifier.
- Sludge from the Clarifier is directed to the Aerobic Digester.
- The Aerobic Digester sends sludge to the Sludge Drying Beds, where dry sludge is sent to landfill.
- The Standby Clarifier and Standby Clarifier are connected to the Clarifier and Aeration Basin.
- The Aeration Basin is aerated by blowers and connected to the Hydro Screen.
- Screenings from the Hydro Screen are sent to landfill.
- The Lift Station manages influent flow into the Aeration Basin.

# APPENDIX "A"  
## Manhole Safety

There are four hazardous gasses that can be produced in a sewer system that can accumulate in any unventilated air space such as manholes or lift station wet wells. These gasses are ammonia, hydrogen sulfide, methane and carbon dioxide. Ammonia and hydrogen sulfide are highly toxic if inhaled. Methane if inhaled is nauseating; however, its primary danger is due to explosive characteristics. Carbon dioxide is an asphixiant.

When it becomes necessary for an operator to enter unventilated manholes, the cover should be removed and the manhole allowed to sufficiently ventilate before entering. The operator should not enter such areas without wearing a harness attached to a safety line with two able bodied men securing the rope above the manhole. Should the operator become affected by one of the above gasses, it will be impossible for him to climb unaided to surface before he becomes unconscious or totally uncoordinated. Explosion proof lighting equipment should be used in such areas and under no circumstances should smoking be permitted.

The most dangerous location on the entire system is the lift station wet well. Any time that it becomes necessary to enter the wet well, the operator should follow the same procedures that were described above. In addition, the operator should wear a self-contained breathing apparatus, also known as an air pack.

# September 20, 1991

**TO:** Al Bolinger, WQFAP  
**FROM:** Richard A. Koch, ERO  
**SUBJECT:** TEKOA O&M MANUAL  

I would like to see several changes in the O&M manual if you agree.

1. The first thing I noted is that WAC 173-240-030(f) requires submission of a complete operation and maintenance manual. An addendum is not a complete manual. This addendum is not even complete; the only appendix transmitted is A.

2. The addendum is also referred to as an appendix in the introduction. It should consistently be called an addendum assuming it is acceptable to us.

3. The list of appendices should be up front with the table of contents, not in the back.

4. Aerobic Digester section says to allow supernatant to overflow into the pump station wetwell. However, Figure 3 shows the overflow going to the chlorine contact chamber. The 1974 plans show that line being plugged off.

5. Polymer Feed System, the manufacturers' representatives did several batch tests to determine a recommended dosage. This section doesn't seem to acknowledge that or refer to the recommended dosage from those tests.

6. Because it is recommended that the polymer feed dose be prepared before wasting sludge, the polymer feed system section should probably precede the aerobic digester section.

7. Section 2.8 the intake fan delivers 300 cfm not cfs.

8. I believe it is more accurate to say the automatic flow proportional controller is regulated by the signal from the flow meter, not merely activated by it.

9. No guidance is given for adjusting the SO2 feed rate to ensure dechlorination and permit compliance. The detection limit for chlorine is above the threshold for toxic effects. It is better for the operator to monitor for SO2 to adjust the dosage for dechlorination.

10. This section should also advise the operator that chlorine is very corrosive in a moist environment. Care should be taken to keep equipment dry and keep caps on to seal out atmospheric moisture.

11. Section 2.9 Yard Hydrants, should this section explain how to purge the vacuum breaker? I would rather see the explanation.

12. Maintenance section, figure 1. There is not much differentiation between the dashed line for sludge and the dashed line for decant. Dots probably would have been a better choice.

13. The engineering report states that the collection system is prone to I/I and even though corrections have been made in the past, shortly thereafter new I/I problems arise. Why then does the O&M report only suggest minimal inspections and action only after significant problems are identified? Preventative inspections and actions will save money and effort.

14. Original design population was 1000, O&M manual table III lists current population of 850 for the design population. Wyatt-Jaykim’s engineering report, section 3.1, of 1988 also uses a design population of 1000.

15. The old O&M manual gives 40,000 gallons as the volume of the aeration basin and 35,000 gallons as the volume of the aerobic digester. The new O&M manual and the design criteria on the plan give 47,000 gallons for volume of aeration basin and 12,000 gallons for volume of digester. I have found no changes identified that explain the volume change in the region’s records. The O&M should have one set of correct volumes and a solids management plan based on the actual volume of solids and recommended MCRT. I agree with 47,000 gal. for aeration basin and 36,500 gal. for digester.

---

I know many towns do television inspections of the collection system periodically and have the lines cleaned with a water jet or other apparatus. An O&M manual should not overlook these procedures. Once the Washington Rural Water Association is able to purchase a television inspection camera and rig the cost will be reasonable for towns like Tekoa.

Appendix "A" Manhole Safety omits the likelihood that a manhole will need to be ventilated with a fan and to supply fresh air. The manhole would become sufficiently ventilated simply by taking a manhole cover up.
